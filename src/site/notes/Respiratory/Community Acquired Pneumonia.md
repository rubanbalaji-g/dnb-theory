## Definition And Epidemiology

- Community acquired pneumonia represents an acute infection of the lung parenchyma.
- It occurs in previously healthy children and is acquired outside of hospital settings.
- The definition excludes children hospitalized within 14 days prior to symptom onset.
- It excludes patients with immunodeficiency, severe malnutrition, and post-measles states.
- It acts as the leading infectious cause of death globally in children under five years of age.
- Mortality is closely linked to poverty, with over 99% of deaths occurring in low and middle-income nations.
- Incidence has declined due to widespread use of antibiotics and vaccines.
- Key protective vaccines include measles, pertussis, _Haemophilus influenzae_ type b (Hib), and pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PCV).

## Pathogenesis And Anatomical Classification

- The disease begins with the disruption of the complex lower respiratory ecosystem.
- Pathogens typically enter via droplet infection.
- Viral infections spread along the respiratory epithelium.
- This leads to airway obstruction from swelling, abnormal secretions, and cellular debris.
- Bacterial pathogens multiply directly within the alveoli.
- This multiplication produces an inflammatory exudate leading to alveolar consolidation.
- Anatomical classification includes lobar pneumonia, lobular pneumonia (bronchopneumonia), and interstitial pneumonia.

## Clinical Presentation

### Symptomatology And Signs

- The illness often begins with an upper respiratory tract infection prodrome featuring rhinitis and cough.
- Tachypnea serves as the most consistent and sensitive clinical manifestation.
- Respiratory distress is evidenced by intercostal, subcostal, or suprasternal retractions.
- Accessory muscle use, nasal flaring, and grunting may be present.
- Auscultation frequently reveals crackles, wheezing, bronchial breathing, and diminished air entry.
- Severe disease manifests with cyanosis, lethargy, tachycardia, and hypoxemia.
- Patients may complain of abdominal pain due to referred pain from lower lobe involvement.

|Clinical Feature|Viral Pneumonia|Typical Bacterial Pneumonia|Atypical Pneumonia|
|---|---|---|---|

|Fever|Low-grade.|High-grade.|Low-grade.|

## Triaging And Admission Criteria

- Assess airway, breathing, and circulation upon arrival.
- Hospitalization is indicated for any infant under three to six months of age.
- Admit patients exhibiting hypoxemia, defined as oxygen saturation below 90-92% on room air.
- Admit children demonstrating a toxic appearance, lethargy, or unconsciousness.
- Severe respiratory distress, intermittent apnea, and grunting mandate admission.
- Inability to tolerate oral fluids or feed, persistent vomiting, and severe dehydration require inpatient care.
- Presence of complicated pneumonia, such as effusion, empyema, or abscess, requires admission.
- Failure of outpatient therapy within 48 to 72 hours warrants hospitalization.

## Diagnostic Evaluation

### Laboratory Investigations

- Community acquired pneumonia is primarily a clinical diagnosis.
- Routine investigations are not required in an outpatient setting.
- Hospitalized children require a complete blood count and blood cultures.
- Viral infections typically show a normal or slightly elevated white blood cell count ($\le$ 20,000/mm³) with lymphocyte predominance.
- Bacterial etiologies yield white blood cell counts of 15,000 to 40,000/mm³ with polymorphonuclear predominance.
- Inflammatory markers like C-reactive protein and procalcitonin are elevated in bacterial disease and aid in monitoring treatment response.
- Molecular multiplex reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction panels identify specific viral pathogens.
- Isotype enzyme linked immunosorbent assay is superior to cold agglutinins for _Mycoplasma_ detection.
- Pulse oximetry remains essential for assessing severity and monitoring therapy.

### Imaging Modalities

- Routine chest radiographs are not required for uncomplicated outpatient cases.
- Radiographs identify consolidation, atelectasis, hyperinflation, and pleural effusions.
- The presence of <span style="color:rgb(255, 138, 117)">pneumatoceles suggests <i>Staphylococcus</i> or <i>Klebsiella</i> infections.</span>
- Chest ultrasonography serves as a highly sensitive point of care tool to identify consolidations and effusions.
- Contrast enhanced computed tomography provides superior imaging for identifying necrosis, abscesses, or underlying anatomical defects.

## Differential Diagnosis

- Recurrent pneumonia should be considered if there are $\ge$ 2 episodes per year or $\ge$ 3 episodes in a lifetime with radiographic clearing between episodes.
- Hereditary causes include [[Respiratory/Cystic Fibrosis\|cystic fibrosis]] and sickle cell disease.
- Immunodeficiency disorders like HIV, chronic granulomatous disease, and severe combined immunodeficiency present similarly.
- Ciliary disorders such as primary ciliary dyskinesia should be excluded.
- Anatomic malformations include pulmonary sequestration and congenital pulmonary airway malformations.
- Acquired causes include retained foreign bodies and severe gastroesophageal reflux disease.

| Protocol | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Transition to Oral Antibiotics** | Occurs upon clinical improvement and once the child is afebrile for 48 to 72 hours. |

### Supportive Care

- Provide continuous oxygen therapy to maintain oxygen saturation above 94%.
- Administer intravenous fluids to address severe distress or clinical dehydration.
- Use Paracetamol as an antipyretic for fever control and comfort.
- Provide advanced respiratory support such as bubble continuous positive airway pressure, high flow nasal cannula, or mechanical ventilation for impending respiratory failure.

## Complications

- Local and intrathoracic complications include pleural effusion, empyema, and pyopneumothorax.
- Pneumatocele formation, lung abscess, necrotizing pneumonia, and bronchopleural fistulas may occur.
- Systemic and metastatic complications encompass bacteremia and overwhelming sepsis.
- Infection may seed to remote locations causing purulent pericarditis, meningitis, suppurative arthritis, and osteomyelitis.

## Prevention Strategies

- Timely immunization remains the most effective preventive measure.
- Ensure administration of pneumococcal conjugate vaccine, _Haemophilus influenzae_ type b vaccine, influenza vaccine, measles vaccine, and pertussis vaccine.
- High-risk infants may receive Nirsevimab for passive immunoprophylaxis against respiratory syncytial virus.
- Environmental measures include promoting exclusive breastfeeding and preventing exposure to passive tobacco smoke.
- Addressing household crowding and providing adequate zinc supplementation significantly mitigates disease risk.
