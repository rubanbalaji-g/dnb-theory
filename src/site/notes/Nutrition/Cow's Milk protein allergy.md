## Definition and Pathophysiology

### Definition
Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA) is an immunologically mediated adverse reaction to one or more proteins present in cow's milk (primarily caseins and whey proteins like beta-lactoglobulin and alpha-lactalbumin). It is distinct from non-immunological cow's milk intolerance (e.g., lactose intolerance).

### Pathophysiology
CMPA is categorized into three distinct immunological mechanisms:

- **IgE-Mediated:** Type I hypersensitivity reaction. Exposure triggers cross-linking of surface IgE on mast cells and basophils, causing immediate degranulation and release of vasoactive amines (histamine, leukotrienes). 
- **Non-IgE-Mediated:** Type IV cell-mediated hypersensitivity or localized T-cell and eosinophilic infiltration. Driven by antigen-specific T-cells causing subacute or chronic localized inflammation, primarily within the gastrointestinal tract.
- **Mixed (IgE and Non-IgE) Mechanism:** Features overlap of both immediate and delayed cellular inflammatory responses.
## Clinical Features and Phenotypes

### Presentation Timelines
- **Immediate Reactions (IgE-mediated):** Occur within minutes up to 2 hours post-ingestion.
- **Delayed Reactions (Non-IgE mediated):** Occur hours to days (up to 1–2 weeks) following chronic ingestion.

### Target Organ Manifestations
- **Gastrointestinal:** Vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain/infantile colic, hematochezia (blood in stools), dysphagia, food refusal, failure to thrive (FTT), and perianal erythema.
- **Cutaneous:** Acute urticaria, angioedema, atopic dermatitis (moderate to severe, subacute or chronic flare-ups).
- **Respiratory:** Rhinorrhea, chronic wheezing, stridor, persistent dry cough. Anaphylaxis represents the severe, life-threatening systemic manifestation.

### Recognized Non-IgE/Mixed Phenotypes
- **Food Protein-Induced Allergic Proctocolitis (FPIAP):** Typically presents in healthy-appearing, breastfed or formula-fed young infants (1–4 months) with blood-streaked, mucoid stools without systemic illness or growth failure.
- **Food Protein-Induced Enteropathy (FPIE):** Characterized by chronic diarrhea, malabsorption, steatorrhea, iron deficiency anemia, and failure to thrive.
- **Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome (FPIES):** A severe phenotype presenting 1–4 hours post-ingestion with profuse, repetitive projectile vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy, dehydration, and systemic hypotension mimicking septic shock.
- **Eosinophilic Gastrointestinal Disorders (EGIDs):** Eosinophilic Esophagitis (EoE) or Gastroenteritis, presenting with food impaction, reflux refractory to acid suppression, and dysphagia.

## Diagnostic Evaluation

### Clinical Diagnostics
Diagnosis rests heavily on a detailed clinical history, physical examination, and structured elimination-challenge protocols.

### Diagnostic Algorithms by Mechanism
- **For Suspected IgE-Mediated CMPA:**
    - *Skin Prick Testing (SPT):* Demonstrates localized wheal-and-flare response; high negative predictive value (>95%).
    - *Serum Specific IgE (sIgE) Quantification:* ImmunoCap assays measuring circulating IgE levels against whole cow's milk extract or specific molecular components ($nBos\ d\ 8$ [casein], $nBos\ d\ 4$, $nBos\ d\ 5$).
- **For Suspected Non-IgE-Mediated CMPA:**
    - SPT and sIgE are characteristically negative.
    - Diagnostic relies strictly on a **Diagnostic Elimination Diet** followed by an **Oral Food Challenge (OFC)**.

### Reference Standard Test
- **Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Food Challenge (DBPCFC):** The diagnostic gold standard. 
- *In clinical practice:* An open oral food challenge (OFC) is typically used after a 2-to-4 week elimination period, provided it is performed under strict medical supervision equipped to manage anaphylaxis.

### Supplementary Investigations
- **Stool Examination:** To document occult/gross blood, fecal leukocytes, or elevated fecal calprotectin (non-specific marker of bowel inflammation).
- **Endoscopy and Biopsy:** Reserved for atypical cases, suspected FPIE, or EoE. Histology reveals mucosal inflammation, villous atrophy, or intraepithelial eosinophilic infiltration (>15 eosinophils per high-power field in EoE).

## Therapeutic Management

### General Dietary Principles
Absolute avoidance of cow's milk protein from the infant's diet. If the infant is exclusively breastfed, the mother must implement a strict cow's milk-free diet, supplemented with calcium (1000 mg/day) and Vitamin D.
