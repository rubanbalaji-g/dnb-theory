## Definition
Post-Polio Syndrome (PPS) is a clinical condition characterized by the new onset of neuromuscular symptoms, primarily weakness, fatigue, and pain, occurring years or decades (typically 15 to 30 years) after the acute episode of paralytic poliomyelitis, in patients who had previously achieved a period of stable neurologic and functional recovery.

## Pathophysiology
The exact etiology remains definitively unproven, but the most widely accepted mechanism is the **"Overuse and Premature Exhaustion"** of enlarged motor units.
* **Acute Phase:** Poliovirus destroys anterior horn cells, leading to muscle denervation.
* **Recovery Phase:** Surviving motor neurons undergo terminal axonal sprouting to re-innervate orphaned muscle fibers, creating "giant motor units" (one neuron supplying up to 5-10 times the normal number of muscle fibers).
* **Late Phase (PPS):** The massive metabolic demand placed on the neuronal cell body to maintain this extensive axonal network eventually leads to premature exhaustion.
* **Distal Degeneration:** This results in distal terminal pruning, denervation of muscle fibers, and the clinical manifestation of new weakness and fatigue.
* **Other proposed mechanisms:** Persistent low-grade viral infection (viral RNA persistence), chronic immune-mediated inflammation, and normal aging superimposed on a depleted motor neuron pool.

## Diagnostic Criteria (March of Dimes / Halstead Criteria)
A diagnosis of PPS requires the presence of all the following criteria:
1. **Prior Paralytic Poliomyelitis:** Evidence of prior anterior horn cell loss (history, residual weakness, atrophy, or electromyographic evidence of denervation/reinnervation).
2. **Stable Interval:** A period of partial or complete functional recovery followed by an extended interval (usually >15 years) of stable neurologic function.
3. **New Onset Symptoms:** Gradual or sudden onset of progressive and persistent new muscle weakness or abnormal muscle fatigability (with or without generalized fatigue, muscle atrophy, or pain).
4. **Duration:** Symptoms must persist for at least one year.
5. **Exclusion:** Exclusion of other neurologic, medical, or orthopedic problems that could explain the symptoms.

## Clinical Features
* **New Muscle Weakness:** Asymmetrical, progressive; typically affects previously involved muscles but can involve muscles previously thought to be unaffected. Bulbar and respiratory muscle involvement can occur.
* **Abnormal Fatigability:** Overwhelming exhaustion out of proportion to the physical exertion; described as a "hitting the wall" sensation. Delay in recovery after physical activity.
* **Muscle Atrophy:** Slow, progressive wasting corresponding to the newly denervated muscles.
* **Pain:**
  * *Muscular Pain:* Deep aching, cramping, or fasciculations.
  * *Joint Pain:* Biomechanical pain secondary to long-term joint instability, abnormal biomechanics, and skeletal deformities (scoliosis, genu recurvatum).
* **Cold Intolerance:** Vasomotor sympathetic dysfunction in affected limbs.
* **Sleep Disturbances:** Sleep apnea (central or obstructive) and hypoventilation, especially if there is brainstem or respiratory muscle involvement.

## Investigations
PPS is primarily a diagnosis of exclusion. Investigations aim to confirm prior polio and rule out alternative etiologies (e.g., radiculopathy, spinal stenosis, motor neuron disease, myopathy).
* **Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS):**
  * *Findings:* Chronic anterior horn cell disease with ongoing active denervation.
  * Giant, polyphasic motor unit action potentials (MUAPs) with reduced recruitment.
  * Fibrillation potentials and positive sharp waves (indicating active denervation).
  * Normal sensory nerve conduction.
* **Neuroimaging (MRI Spine):** To exclude compressive myelopathy, spinal stenosis, or structural anomalies.
* **Laboratory Tests:**
  * Creatine Kinase (CK): Normal or mildly elevated (due to muscle overuse/denervation).
  * Routine bloods (CBC, TFTs, ESR/CRP, Vitamin B12, Vitamin D) to rule out systemic causes of fatigue and weakness.
* **Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs) & Sleep Study:** Indicated if there is clinical suspicion of respiratory muscle weakness or sleep-disordered breathing.

## Management
Management is multidisciplinary, symptomatic, and rehabilitative. There is no curative or specific disease-modifying pharmacotherapy.
* **Energy Conservation and Pacing:** The cornerstone of management. Breaking tasks into smaller segments, scheduling rest periods, and avoiding muscle exhaustion.
* **Exercise Prescription:**
  * *Non-fatiguing Exercise:* Submaximal, aerobic conditioning, and gentle stretching.
  * Intense, fatiguing resistive exercises are strictly contraindicated as they accelerate motor unit destruction.
  * Aquatic therapy (warm water) is highly beneficial.
* **Orthoses and Assistive Devices:**
  * Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs), knee-ankle-foot orthoses (KAFOs), canes, or wheelchairs to improve mobility, correct biomechanics, and unload painful, unstable joints.
* **Pain Management:** NSAIDs, physical modalities (heat, massage), and neuropathic pain agents (gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline) if indicated.
* **Respiratory Support:** Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (CPAP/BiPAP) for sleep apnea or chronic hypoventilation.
* **Weight Management:** To reduce mechanical stress on weakened muscles and joints.
* **Pharmacological Trials:** IVIG, pyridostigmine, modafinil, and lamotrigine have been studied for fatigue and weakness but lack robust evidence for routine recommendation.

## Prognosis
* PPS is a slowly progressive disorder with periods of relative stability interspersed with decline.
* The rate of decline in muscle strength is typically slow (1-2% per year).
* It is rarely life-threatening, except in cases of severe progressive bulbar or respiratory muscle involvement.
