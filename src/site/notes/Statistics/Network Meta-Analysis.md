### Definition and Core Concept

- A network meta-analysis is an advanced statistical method designed to extend the capabilities of a conventional meta-analysis.
- It is utilized to simultaneously combine and summarize both direct and indirect evidence concerning multiple treatments for a given clinical condition.
- This methodology is particularly necessary and useful when the specific set of evaluated interventions differs across the various individual studies being analyzed.

### Methodological Advantages

- A key mechanism of a network meta-analysis is its ability to "borrow strength" from the available indirect evidence across the network of included trials.
- By incorporating this indirect evidence alongside direct comparisons, there is a substantial potential gain in the mathematical precision of the resulting statistical estimates.
- Because of this comprehensive synthesis of data, the treatment effect estimates produced may be significantly less biased.
- Overall, the conclusions and effect estimates derived from a network meta-analysis are considered to be more robust.

### Relationship to Conventional Meta-Analysis

- A network meta-analysis builds upon the foundational principles of a conventional meta-analysis, which is a statistical technique used to combine the findings from several selected studies to provide a single summary estimate of a specific treatment effect.
- The primary advantage of any meta-analysis is the pooling of results from multiple studies to increase the available sample size, reduce the standard error, improve precision, and increase the overall statistical power.
- While a conventional meta-analysis typically focuses on directly comparing two specific interventions (such as a single drug versus a placebo) across head-to-head trials, a network meta-analysis extends this by allowing researchers to evaluate and compare multiple different treatments simultaneously, even if they have not all been directly compared to one another in primary trials.
