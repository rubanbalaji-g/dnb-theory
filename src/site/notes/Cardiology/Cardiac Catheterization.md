## Introduction and Historical Perspective

- Diagnostic and interventional cardiac catheterization continues to be an unparalleled and valuable tool in the assessment and management of congenital and acquired heart diseases in children.
- The concept of cardiac catheterization was first introduced by Claude Bernard in 1844, who inserted a mercury temperature probe into the carotid artery of a horse to measure central temperature.
- In 1929, Dr. Werner Forssmann self-experimented by inserting a urological catheter into his own antecubital vein and advancing it to the right atrium, proving its feasibility and earning him the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1956.
- Diagnostic cardiac catheterization was subsequently refined and established in clinical practice by André Cournand and Dickinson Richards in the 1940s, with pediatric applications rapidly expanding thereafter.

## Indications for Cardiac Catheterization

- While noninvasive imaging modalities (echocardiography, computed tomography, and magnetic resonance imaging) have largely replaced diagnostic catheterization for basic anatomic definition, catheterization remains vital for specific diagnostic and therapeutic purposes.

|Category|Specific Indications|
|:--|:--|
|**Hemodynamic Assessment**|Obtaining invasive data (pressures, vascular resistances, blood flow volumes, Qp:Qs) to assess surgical suitability. Evaluation of pulmonary hypertension (PHTN) and pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR). Assessment between stages of complex congenital heart disease repair (e.g., prior to Fontan procedure).|
|**Anatomic Definition**|Defining structures when noninvasive imaging is suboptimal (e.g., major aortopulmonary collaterals, right ventricle-dependent coronary circulation). Post-heart transplant surveillance for coronary vasculopathy.|
|**Preferred Interventions**|Percutaneous closure of patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), atrial septal defects (ASD), and muscular ventricular septal defects (VSD). Right ventricle endomyocardial biopsy in post-heart transplant patients. Balloon valvuloplasty for pulmonary and aortic valve stenosis.|
|**Alternative Interventions**|Interventions in high-risk surgical patients, such as transcatheter superior sinus venosus ASD closure in patients with PHTN.|

## Pre-Procedure Preparation and Planning

### History and Physical Examination

- An accurate history must be obtained, including the patient's initial and current cardiac anatomy, previous surgeries, and prior catheterization reports.
- Specific inquiries should include a history of bleeding disorders, contrast reactions, drug allergies, concurrent renal insufficiency, and current medications.
- For female patients of childbearing age, a pregnancy test and inquiry regarding the last menstrual period are mandatory prior to the procedure.
- A thorough cardiovascular physical examination is essential to assess for cyanosis, weak pulses, delayed capillary refill, and superficial venous collaterals.
- Inspection of the planned vascular entry site directs the operator to alter the access plan if adequate pulsatility is lacking or extensive scarring is present.

### Procedure Planning and Consent

- A comprehensive procedure plan must be generated, including anticipated access sites, sheath sizes, necessary equipment, and the specific sequence of the procedure.
- Informed consent must detail general complications (bleeding, vascular injury, clot formation) and procedure-specific risks (e.g., device embolization in ASD closure, valve regurgitation or "suicidal right ventricle" in pulmonary valvuloplasty).
- Blood typing and screening should be obtained for all patients, with blood products readily available at the procedure location for high-risk cases.

## Sedation, Anesthesia, and Pharmacology

- Most pediatric cardiac catheterization procedures require the involvement of anesthesiologists with expertise in pediatric cardiology.
- General anesthesia with mechanical ventilation is generally preferred in children to facilitate procedural safety, secure the airway, and alleviate anxiety, especially during precise interventional procedures.
- In adults or cooperative older children, local anesthesia with minimal sedation is preferred to obtain the most reliable hemodynamic data reflecting a "steady state" with spontaneous breathing.
- Pre-procedure medications (midazolam, morphine, fentanyl, dexmedetomidine) reduce anxiety, promote amnesia, and facilitate parental separation.
- Ketamine is advantageous in patients with Tetralogy of Fallot physiology, as it increases systemic vascular resistance, mitigating the risk of right-to-left shunting and hypercyanotic spells.
- Propofol must be avoided in patients with systolic dysfunction due to its profound negative inotropic effects on the myocardium.
- Lidocaine, the most frequently used local anesthetic, should be buffered with sodium bicarbonate and administered with a small needle (24- or 25-gauge) to minimize burning.

## Vascular Access Routes

- Ultrasound (US) guidance is routinely utilized in modern catheterization laboratories to improve access success rates and decrease site-related complications, especially in small infants with a narrow margin of error.

|Access Route|Characteristics and Indications|
|:--|:--|
|**Femoral Vein and Artery**|The most common access sites. The femoral artery lies lateral to the femoral vein. Ultrasound differentiates the pulsatile artery from the compressible, laminar-flow vein.|
|**Internal Jugular Vein (IJV)**|The common carotid artery lies medial to the IJV. The right IJV provides a straight trajectory to the heart and is preferred for superior cavopulmonary anastomosis (Glenn) assessment, RV endomyocardial biopsy, and apical muscular VSD closure.|
|**Common Carotid / Axillary Artery**|Desirable for specific procedures requiring an ideal trajectory, such as PDA stent placement, aortic balloon valvuloplasty, and modified Blalock-Taussig shunt interventions in small infants.|
|**Umbilical Vein**|Useful in neonates as it provides a straight course to the right atrium before the ductus venosus closes. Typically used for balloon atrial septostomy (Rashkind procedure).|

## Catheters and Hemodynamic Measurements

### Catheter Types

- **End-hole Catheters:** Examples include balloon-tipped Berman wedge catheters and Goodale Lubin catheters. They possess a single hole at the tip, making them ideal for measuring specific localized pressures (including wedge pressures) and performing selective hand-injection angiograms.
- **Multi-hole Catheters:** Examples include Berman angiographic, NIH, and Pigtail catheters. They feature multiple side holes, allowing for the delivery of large volumes of contrast at rapid flow rates without inducing catheter recoil or injection-related arrhythmias. They cannot be used to measure wedge pressures.

### Hemodynamic Data Acquisition

- Accurate hemodynamic recording is crucial and includes measurement of oxygen saturations and pressure recordings to calculate pulmonary cardiac output (Qp), systemic cardiac output (Qs), the Qp:Qs ratio, and indexed pulmonary vascular resistance (PVRi).
- The Fick principle is utilized to calculate cardiac output by measuring oxygen consumption and the arteriovenous oxygen concentration difference.
- Thermodilution, utilizing a flow-directed Swan-Ganz catheter, measures cardiac output by injecting room-temperature saline and detecting downstream temperature changes, primarily in patients without intracardiac shunts.
- To mitigate the effects of respiration, pressure measurements should be recorded at the end of passive exhalation when intrathoracic pressure is most stable.
- In mechanically ventilated patients receiving positive pressure ventilation, the lower recorded pressure values during the respiratory cycle are conventionally used.
- The system must be meticulously de-aired, and transducers must be leveled to the mid-left atrium (mid-axillary line) and "zeroed" to atmospheric pressure to ensure accuracy.

## Angiography and Transcatheter Interventions

### Angiography Principles

- The "As Low As Reasonably Achievable" (ALARA) principle must be adopted for all X-ray-based imaging to minimize radiation exposure.
- Hypertonic radiopaque contrast agents can cause transient myocardial depression, hypotension, followed by tachycardia and a shift of interstitial fluid into the circulation, potentially exacerbating heart failure in critically ill patients.
- All catheters must be aspirated prior to contrast injection to ensure they are de-aired and not wedged against a vessel wall, which could cause life-threatening dissection or myocardial staining.

### Interventional Procedures

- **Balloon Valvuloplasty:** Considered the first-line treatment for isolated congenital pulmonary or aortic valve stenosis. A balloon is inflated across the stenotic valve until the "waist" disappears, tearing the fused commissures.
- **Transcatheter Valve Implantation:** Percutaneous placement of a stent-mounted bioprosthetic valve (e.g., Melody or Harmony valve) in the pulmonary position is routinely performed for patients with residual pulmonary insufficiency following prior Tetralogy of Fallot repair.
- **Septostomy:** The Rashkind balloon atrial septostomy is a lifesaving intervention used to create or enlarge an atrial communication, predominantly in neonates with Transposition of the Great Arteries to ensure adequate mixing of systemic and pulmonary venous blood.
- **Device Closure:** The catheterization laboratory is the standard setting for the closure of ASDs, PDAs, and selected muscular VSDs using specialized occlusion devices and coils.

## Complications and Management

- The overall complication rate for pediatric cardiac catheterization is estimated at 7.8% (6% minor and 1.8% major complications), with major complications occurring more frequently during interventional procedures.

|Complication|Pathophysiology and Management Strategy|
|:--|:--|
|**Arrhythmias**|Induced by catheter/wire contact with the endocardium. Usually transient. Sustained ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation requires immediate cardioversion/defibrillation. Patients with ventricular inversion (L-TGA) are at high risk for complete heart block during right heart catheterization.|
|**Cardiac Perforation**|A rare but life-threatening complication, most commonly occurring at the atrial appendage or right ventricular outflow tract. Results in cardiac tamponade. Managed with immediate echocardiographic confirmation, pericardiocentesis, reversal of anticoagulation, and potential autotransfusion from the pericardial drain to the venous sheath.|
|**Arterial Thrombosis (Pulse Loss)**|Occurs in approximately 5.1% of cases. Risk factors include weight <5 kg, long/large sheaths, and small femoral arteries (<3 mm). Management includes immediate intravenous heparin and close perfusion monitoring. Refractory cases may require recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (rTPA) or mechanical recanalization.|
|**Air Embolism**|Air introduced via un-flushed sheaths, ruptured balloons, or negative intrathoracic pressure. Prevented by the "Canadian technique" (withdrawing wires through a saline-filled syringe to create a fluid seal) and using carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) instead of air for balloon inflation.|
|**Contrast Nephropathy**|Caused by excessive contrast administration. High-risk patients (pre-existing renal insufficiency) require meticulous prehydration protocols and strict limitation of total contrast volume.|
