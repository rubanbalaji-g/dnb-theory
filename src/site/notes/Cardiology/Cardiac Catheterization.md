## Indications for Cardiac Catheterization

- Diagnostic cardiac catheterization is utilized to obtain invasive hemodynamic data (such as pressures, vascular resistances, and blood flow volumes) necessary to assess surgical suitability, such as prior to a Fontan procedure or in patients with pulmonary hypertension.
- It provides precise anatomic definition when noninvasive imaging is suboptimal, particularly for evaluating major aortopulmonary collateral arteries (MAPCAs) or coronary artery anatomy.
- It is used to evaluate complex congenital heart disease, such as tetralogy of Fallot with pulmonary atresia, pulmonary atresia with intact ventricular septum and coronary sinusoids, and hypoplastic left heart syndrome with mitral stenosis.
- Therapeutic or interventional catheterization has become the preferred approach for specific defects, providing outcomes similar to or better than surgery.
- Common transcatheter interventions include patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) closure, atrial septal defect (ASD) closure, and right ventricular endomyocardial biopsies post-heart transplantation.
- Balloon valvuloplasty serves as the first-line treatment for isolated congenital pulmonary and aortic valve stenosis.
- Stent placements are routinely performed for recoarctation of the aorta and branch pulmonary artery stenosis.
- Balloon atrial septostomy (Rashkind procedure) is a life-saving intervention used to create an atrial communication in neonates with transposition of the great arteries.

## Pre-procedure Preparation

- A detailed history must be obtained, focusing on the patient's initial cardiac anatomy, previous surgical or catheterization reports, anesthesia complications, bleeding disorders, and contrast allergies.
- The physical examination must assess for cyanosis, weak pulses, and superficial venous collaterals, which may dictate the choice of vascular access or highlight specific hemodynamic aberrations.
- Routine pre-catheterization assessment includes blood chemistry, complete blood count, urinalysis, chest X-ray, electrocardiogram, and echocardiogram.
- Routine blood and urine cultures are not indicated prior to the procedure.
- Prophylactic antibiotic coverage is strictly indicated for interventional procedures where foreign materials (such as devices or stents) are being inserted.
- Informed consent must detail general procedural risks as well as intervention-specific complications, such as device embolization, heart block, or cardiac erosion.

## Vascular Access and Equipment

- Ultrasound-guided vascular access is routinely employed to improve success rates and decrease access site-related complications, a practice that is especially critical in small infants and neonates where the margin of error is narrow.

|Access Site / Catheter Type|Clinical Application and Features|
|:--|:--|
|**Femoral Vein and Artery**|The standard access route for the majority of pediatric catheterizations.|
|**Internal Jugular Vein (IJV)**|Preferred for right ventricular endomyocardial biopsy, accessing the pulmonary artery in single-ventricle patients post-Glenn shunt, or when femoral veins are occluded.|
|**End-Hole Catheters**|Examples include Berman wedge and Glide catheters; ideal for measuring localized pressures, obtaining wedge pressures, and performing hand-injected selective angiograms.|
|**Multi-Hole Catheters**|Examples include Pigtail and NIH catheters; optimal for high-volume, rapid-flow contrast injections (angiography) as they prevent catheter recoil and reduce the risk of injection-induced arrhythmias.|

## Hemodynamic Assessment and Angiography

- Essential hemodynamic data collected includes systemic and pulmonary cardiac output (Qs and Qp), the Qp:Qs ratio, intracardiac pressures, and indexed pulmonary vascular resistance (PVRi).
- Cardiac output and shunt calculations rely on the Fick principle, where flow equals the rate of oxygen consumption (VO2) divided by the change in oxygen content.
- To maximize the accuracy of invasive pressure measurements, the setup should utilize a stiffer catheter, shorter tubing, fewer stopcocks, and a transducer zeroed at the mid-chest (mid-left atrium) level.
- During spontaneously breathing states, pressure tracings should ideally be recorded at end-expiration to minimize intrathoracic pressure variations; however, in intubated patients on positive pressure ventilation, the lower recorded pressure values are conventionally used.
- Angiography must follow the "as low as reasonably achievable" (ALARA) principle for radiation exposure, defaulting to low-level image intensifier modes.
- Maximum total doses of nonionic iso-osmolar contrast should be carefully monitored to mitigate the risk of acute kidney injury, generally avoiding volumes strictly over 6 cc/kg, though complex procedures may necessitate higher amounts.
- Catheters must be aspirated prior to any contrast injection to de-air the system and confirm the tip is not wedged against a cardiac wall, preventing intramural staining or vascular dissection.

## Complications and Management

- The overall complication rate for pediatric cardiac catheterization is approximately 7.8% (6% minor and 1.8% major complications), with major events occurring more frequently during interventional procedures.

|Complication|Pathophysiology and Clinical Management|
|:--|:--|
|**Arrhythmias**|Induced by catheter contact with the endocardium. Manifests as SVT, VT, or VF. Management requires immediate catheter repositioning and readiness for cardioversion/defibrillation. Complete heart block is notably a risk in patients with ventricular inversion (L-TGA).|
|**Access Site Thrombosis**|Manifests as a loss of palpable pulse; overall incidence is 5.1%, with the highest risk in infants under 5 kg. Managed initially with systemic heparinization; refractory cases may require recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (rTPA).|
|**Cardiac Perforation**|Causes sudden hypotension, elevated central venous pressure, and an enlarged cardiac silhouette due to hemopericardium/tamponade. Requires immediate echocardiographic confirmation, reversal of anticoagulation, and emergent pericardiocentesis.|
|**Air Embolism**|Can occur via sheath introduction, ruptured balloons, or negative intrathoracic pressure (e.g., snoring or sudden wire removal). Prevented by using a water seal (Canadian technique), positive pressure ventilation, and keeping sheaths on a continuous flush.|
