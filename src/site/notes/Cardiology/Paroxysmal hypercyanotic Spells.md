## Pathophysiology

- Paroxysmal hypercyanotic attacks (hypoxic, "blue," or "tet" spells) are medical emergencies that usually develop during the first year of life.
- These episodes occur secondary to an acute decrease in pulmonary blood flow, which leads to a drastically increased right-to-left shunt across a ventricular septal defect and profound systemic desaturation.
- The acute reduction in pulmonary blood flow is often triggered by infundibular spasm due to a surge in circulating catecholamines during stress, feeding, or crying.
- Alternatively, the activation of mechanoreceptors in the right or left ventricle can lead to sudden peripheral vasodilatation, causing a fall in systemic vascular resistance that exacerbates the right-to-left shunting of deoxygenated blood.
- Prolonged reduction in pulmonary blood flow results in severe systemic hypoxia and worsening metabolic acidosis, which can perpetuate the cycle of the spell.

## Clinical Features

- Spells predominantly occur in the morning upon initially awakening, or immediately following episodes of vigorous crying or exertion.
- The infant typically cries incessantly, becomes highly irritable, and is clinically inconsolable.
- Hyperpnea and tachypnea are prominent features, manifesting as deep, rapid, and gasping respirations without significant subcostal recession.
- As the spell progresses, clinical cyanosis deepens and the patient may develop generalized weakness.
- In severe, prolonged attacks, the severe hypoxia can lead to syncope, unconsciousness, convulsions, cerebrovascular accidents (stroke), and death.
- Older patients instinctively assume a sitting or squatting posture as soon as they become dyspneic, which temporarily increases systemic vascular resistance and improves pulmonary blood flow.

## Diagnostic Investigations

|Modality|Specific Findings|
|:--|:--|
|**Auscultation**|The hallmark physical finding during an active spell is the temporary disappearance or marked reduction in the intensity of the typical systolic ejection murmur, which occurs because blood flow across the obstructed right ventricular outflow tract diminishes. A normal first heart sound and a single second heart sound are usually appreciated.|
|**Electrocardiogram (ECG)**|Demonstrates right axis deviation accompanied by right ventricular hypertrophy, often manifesting as a pure 'R' wave in lead V1.|
|**Chest Radiograph (CXR)**|Displays a normal-sized heart with an upturned apex and the absence of the main pulmonary artery segment, classically described as the "Coeur en Sabot" (boot-shaped heart) appearance. The pulmonary fields typically appear oligemic due to reduced pulmonary blood flow.|
|**Echocardiography**|Utilized to confirm the underlying disease morphology, evaluating the anatomy of the ventricular septal defect and the degree of right ventricular outflow tract obstruction.|
|**Cardiac Catheterization**|Crossing the right ventricular outflow tract during catheter manipulation can precipitate a cyanotic spell. The pulmonary artery pressure is usually recorded as much lower than normal, typically ranging from 5 to 10 mm Hg.|

## Management

### Immediate Acute Steps

- Check the airway immediately and deliver supplemental oxygen via face mask or nasal cannula, though increasing inspired oxygen will not completely reverse cyanosis caused by fixed intracardiac right-to-left shunting.
- Place the infant in the knee-chest position immediately and ensure that their clothing is not constrictive.
- Administer sedation to calm the infant and reduce catecholamine surges; options include subcutaneous morphine (0.2 mg/kg), intramuscular ketamine (3-5 mg/kg), intranasal fentanyl, or intranasal midazolam.
- Correct hypovolemia to optimize right ventricular preload by administering an intravenous fluid bolus of 10 mL/kg of dextrose normal saline.
- Administer intravenous sodium bicarbonate (1-2 mL/kg, diluted 1:1 or in 10 mL/kg N/5 in 5% dextrose) rapidly to correct metabolic acidosis if the spell is unusually severe and unresponsive to initial positioning.
- Initiate beta-adrenergic blockade to relax the right ventricular outflow tract using intravenous metoprolol (0.1 mg/kg slowly over 5 minutes, repeatable every 5 minutes for a maximum of 3 doses), propranolol (0.15-0.25 mg/kg/dose), or esmolol.

### Refractory Management

- If persistent desaturation occurs with no significant clinical improvement, initiate a vasopressor infusion to acutely increase systemic vascular resistance and decrease right-to-left shunting.
- Vasopressor options include intravenous phenylephrine (5 mcg/kg as a bolus followed by a 1-4 mcg/kg/min continuous infusion) or methoxamine (0.1-0.2 mg/kg/dose IV or 0.1-0.4 mg/kg/dose IM).
- If the spells persist despite maximum pharmacological therapy, the patient should be paralyzed, electively intubated, and mechanically ventilated.
- Any spell-induced seizures must be promptly managed with intravenous diazepam (0.2 mg/kg) or midazolam (0.1-0.2 mg/kg/dose).

### Preventive and Long-Term Management

- Following stabilization, initiate prophylactic oral beta-blocker therapy, such as propranolol (0.5-1.5 mg/kg every 6-8 hours) at the maximally tolerated dose, to improve resting oxygen saturation and decrease the frequency of future spells.
- Conduct a careful neurological examination and obtain CNS imaging if any focal deficits are present following a severe anoxic spell.
- Administer therapeutic iron supplementation if the patient is anemic, as iron deficiency decreases red blood cell deformability and highly exacerbates the risk of cerebrovascular strokes.
- Counsel parents regarding the identification of precipitating factors like dehydration, fever, and pain, and emphasize the necessity of preventing these triggers.
- Because the occurrence of a cyanotic spell represents a critical failure of pulmonary blood flow, it indicates the need for early palliative or corrective cardiac surgery.
