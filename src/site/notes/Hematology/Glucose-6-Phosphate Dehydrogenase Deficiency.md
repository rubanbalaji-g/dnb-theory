## Genetics and Epidemiology

- Most common red blood cell enzyme deficiency globally.
- Affects over 400 million individuals representing 4.9% global prevalence.
- High incidence correlates geographically with malaria endemicity.
- Represents balanced polymorphism conferring evolutionary resistance against falciparum malaria in heterozygous females.
- Inherited as x-linked recessive trait.
- Full disease expression occurs in hemizygous males and homozygous females.
- Heterozygous females exhibit variable intermediate expression due to random x-chromosome inactivation (Lyon hypothesis).
- More than 200 pathogenic variants identified.
- Complete gene deletions remain incompatible with embryonic life due to housekeeping essentiality.
- Missense point mutations cause amino acid substitutions and enzyme destabilization.
- Pathogenic variants near amino terminus associate with milder clinical disease.
- Pathogenic variants near carboxyl terminus affecting nadp-binding sites associate with severe chronic nonspherocytic hemolytic anemia.
- Variant a- affects approximately 13% of african-descent males.
- Variant mediterranean (b-) prevalent in 5-40% of greek, italian, middle eastern, and asian populations.
- Variant canton affects approximately 5% of chinese population.

## Pathophysiology

- Hexose monophosphate shunt relies on glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase.
- Enzyme catalyzes conversion of glucose-6-phosphate to 6-phosphogluconic acid.
- Reaction generates reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (nadph).
- Nadph maintains cellular glutathione in reduced, functional state (gsh).
- Reduced glutathione physiologically inactivates generated oxidant compounds including hydrogen peroxide.
- Enzyme deficiency depletes nadph and reduced glutathione.
- Cellular inability to eliminate oxidants results in oxidative damage to red blood cell membrane sulfhydryl groups.
- Hemoglobin oxidizes to methemoglobin and denatures.
- Denatured hemoglobin precipitates forming intracellular inclusions called heinz bodies.
- Heinz bodies attach to red cell membrane band 3 proteins creating antigenic sites.
- Splenic macrophages identify abnormal membranes and physically pluck out heinz bodies.
- Phagocytosis produces fragmented erythrocytes, bite cells, and blister cells.
- Damaged red cells undergo rapid premature destruction via intravascular and extravascular hemolysis.
- Enzyme activity falls progressively as normal red cells age.
- Variant a- produces age-labile enzyme; older erythrocytes lyse preferentially while younger erythrocytes with residual enzyme survive.
- Age-labile enzyme kinetics produce compensated hemolytic process during continuous drug exposure.

## Clinical Manifestations

### Asymptomatic State

- Majority of deficient individuals remain entirely asymptomatic.
- Clinical illness requires exposure to triggering external oxidants.

### Episodic Acute Hemolytic Anemia

- Triggered by infections (most common), specific medications, or chemical exposures.
- Hemolysis onset typically 24-96 hours post-exposure.
- Features sudden onset pallor, dark urine (hemoglobinuria), fatigue, and jaundice.
- Associated chemical triggers include henna cosmetics and naphthalene (mothballs).

### Favism

- Acute life-threatening hemolytic syndrome caused by fava bean ingestion or pollen exposure.
- Beans contain highly oxidative divicine, isouramil, and convicine producing hydrogen peroxide.
- Associated primarily with mediterranean and canton variants.
- Can progress rapidly to acute renal failure requiring urgent blood transfusion.

### Neonatal Jaundice

- Spontaneous hemolysis and hyperbilirubinemia occur in preterm infants with a- variant.
- Severe hyperbilirubinemia progressing to kernicterus occurs in mediterranean and canton variants.
- Pathogenesis involves concurrent defective enzyme activity in hepatocytes reducing bilirubin glucuronidation.
- Transplacental maternal drug exposure or breast milk transmission can trigger neonatal hemolysis.
- Co-inheritance with ugt1a1 promoter mutations (gilbert syndrome) drastically worsens neonatal jaundice.

### Chronic Nonspherocytic Hemolytic Anemia

- Typically associated with sporadic genetic variants.
- Features chronic continuous red cell destruction without external triggers.
- Manifests with severe transfusion-dependent anemia, reticulocytosis, and mild splenomegaly.
- Slight jaundice and gallstones commonly present.

## Laboratory Investigations

### Primary Hematology

- Hemoglobin and hematocrit demonstrate precipitous fall during acute crises.
- Reticulocyte count heavily elevated indicating compensatory marrow hyperplasia.
- Direct antiglobulin test (coombs) strictly negative.

### Peripheral Blood Smear

- Hallmark bite cells prominently visible.
- Blister cells show hemoglobin puddled to one side.
- Fragmented erythrocytes (schistocytes) and spherocytes present.
- Polychromasia indicates elevated reticulocyte presence.

### Supravital Staining

- Crystal violet or brilliant cresyl blue stains reveal intracellular heinz bodies.
- Heinz bodies visible exclusively within first 3-4 days of acute illness before reticuloendothelial clearance.
- Heinz bodies absent on standard wright-stained blood films.

### Hemolysis Markers

- Haptoglobin severely reduced or absent.
- Hemopexin levels reduced.
- Plasma free hemoglobin elevated (hemoglobinemia).
- Unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin significantly elevated.
- Lactate dehydrogenase markedly increased.
- Urinalysis reveals hemoglobinuria and hemosiderinuria.

### Diagnostic Enzyme Assays

- Screening tests: methylene blue decolorization, methemoglobin reduction test, nadph fluorescence.
- Confirmatory test: quantitative spectrophotometric assay measuring precise nadph formation rate.
- Enzyme activity less than 10% of normal establishes definitive diagnosis.
- **Critical timing caveat:** false normal results frequently occur if tested immediately following acute hemolytic episodes.
- Reticulocytes and young red cells possess maximal enzyme activity; preferential destruction of older cells leaves behind younger cells with falsely reassuring enzyme levels.
- Diagnosis must be suspected if enzyme activity appears low-normal amid profound reticulocytosis.
- Definitive quantitative testing must be deferred several weeks post-crisis until red cell population normalizes.
- Molecular and electrophoretic analysis available for precise variant identification.

## Management

### Preventive Strategies

- Avoidance of deleterious agents remains paramount therapeutic measure.
- Strict dietary prohibition of fava beans.
- Meticulous review of prescribed medications to avoid oxidant drugs.
- Screen high-risk ethnic populations prior to initiating high-risk therapies (e.g., primaquine, rasburicase).
- Educate patients and families to recognize dark urine, pallor, and jaundice during infections.

### Acute Hemolytic Crisis

- Discontinue inciting oxidant agent immediately.
- Maintain hemodynamic stability and strict fluid balance.
- Provide packed red blood cell transfusions for severe symptomatic anemia.
- Specific transfusion indications: hemoglobin falls below 7 g/dl, or persistent hemoglobinuria with hemoglobin below 9 g/dl.
- Complete recovery expected upon removal of oxidant stress.
- Absolute contraindication: avoid methylene blue administration for drug-induced methemoglobinemia; causes lethal severe oxidative hemolysis in enzyme-deficient hosts.

### Neonatal Jaundice

- Treat aggressively utilizing established phototherapy protocols.
- Perform exchange transfusion if bilirubin levels threaten kernicterus.

### Chronic Nonspherocytic Hemolytic Anemia

- Maintain baseline hemoglobin 8-10 g/dl via chronic red cell transfusions.
- Implement intensive iron chelation therapy to combat transfusional hemosiderosis.
- Administer daily folic acid supplementation (1 mg/day) to support hyperactive erythropoiesis.
- Splenectomy provides minimal benefit generally; reserved strictly for severe hypersplenism, unmanageable chronic anemia, or massive splenomegaly causing physical impediment.
- Provide genetic counseling and prenatal diagnosis if mother is known heterozygote carrying severe class I variants.
