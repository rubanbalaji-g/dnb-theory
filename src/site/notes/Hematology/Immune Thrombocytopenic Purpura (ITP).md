## Introduction And Epidemiology

- Previously designated idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura; currently termed immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) due to established autoimmune pathogenesis,.
- Most common etiology of acute onset thrombocytopenia in otherwise-healthy children.
- Incidence ranges between 7.2-9.5 per 100,000 children.
- Peak incidence occurs between 1-7 years of age,,.
- Equal sex distribution observed in young children; higher prevalence noted in adolescent females,.
- Seasonal clustering prominent during late winter and springtime,.

## Pathophysiology And Mechanisms

- Pathogenesis involves immune-mediated platelet destruction combined with impaired platelet production,.
- **Antibody-Mediated Destruction:**
    - Autoantibodies target specific platelet surface glycoproteins, predominantly GPIIb/IIIa, GPIb/IX, and GPIa/IIa complexes,,.
    - Splenic macrophages recognize IgG Fc region of antiplatelet antibodies, leading to phagocytosis and destruction,,.
    - Antibodies against GPIb/IX may induce platelet desialylation, resulting in hepatic clearance via carbohydrate receptors.
- **Impaired Megakaryopoiesis:**
    - Relative decrease in megakaryocyte production contributes significantly to thrombocytopenia.
    - Cytotoxic T-cells and specific immune cytokines impair megakaryocyte function and platelet generation,.
    - Thrombopoietin levels remain minimally elevated, failing to adequately compensate for destruction.
- **Immunologic Dysregulation:**
    - Disease characterized by Th1 dominant proinflammatory cytokine state,.
    - Upregulation of genes mediating cellular toxicity (granzyme B, perforin) in CD3+/CD8+ T-cells.
    - CD4+ T-helper cells stimulate autoantibody-secreting B-cells.
- **Triggering Events:**
    - Acute viral infections frequently precede onset by 1-4 weeks,.
    - Implicated agents include Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), cytomegalovirus (CMV), varicella, rubella, hepatitis C, parvovirus, and severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2),,.
    - Associated with _Helicobacter pylori_ infection and rarely follows measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccination.

## Classification Based On Disease Duration

- **Acute ITP:** Thrombocytopenia resolving within 3 months.
- **Persistent ITP:** Thrombocytopenia lasting 3-12 months.
- **Chronic ITP:** Thrombocytopenia persisting beyond 12 months,.

## Clinical Manifestations And Bleeding Severity

- Patients typically present as previously healthy children with sudden onset bleeding manifestations.
- Characteristic features include generalized petechiae, purpura, and nonpalpable ecchymoses,.
- Mucosal bleeding manifestations occur frequently: epistaxis, hematuria, hematochezia, menometrorrhagia, and oral oozing,,.
- Patients remain afebrile without systemic symptoms.
- Physical examination lacks hepatosplenomegaly, significant lymphadenopathy, bone pain, or pallor (unless significant hemorrhage occurred),.
- Presence of organomegaly strongly suggests alternative diagnoses (leukemia, systemic lupus erythematosus, autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome),.

### Bleeding Severity Grading

|Grade|Clinical Findings|
|:--|:--|
|**No Symptoms**|Thrombocytopenia without clinical bleeding.|
|**Mild Symptoms**|Bruising, petechiae, occasional minor epistaxis; minimal interference with daily activities.|
|**Moderate Symptoms**|Severe skin/mucosal lesions, troublesome epistaxis, menorrhagia.|
|**Severe Symptoms**|Bleeding requiring transfusion or hospitalization (melena, profound epistaxis); seriously limits quality of life.|

### Intracranial Hemorrhage (ICH)

- Rarest but most severe complication; incidence <1% (approximately 0.2-0.8%),,.
- Often occurs within 1 week of diagnosis, but 30% occur >6 months post-diagnosis.
- Platelet count <10,000/μL in 75% of ICH cases.
- Identifiable risk factors: Head trauma, concurrent hematuria, severe generalized hemorrhage, arteriovenous malformations, aspirin exposure.
- Anatomical sites: Intracerebral (77%, predominantly supratentorial) and subdural (23%).

## Diagnostic Evaluation

- Diagnosis remains primarily one of exclusion.
- **Complete Blood Count (CBC):** Isolated severe thrombocytopenia (<100,000/μL; often <20,000/μL),. Normal hemoglobin, white blood cell (WBC) count, and differential.
- **Peripheral Blood Smear:** Large platelets (macrothrombocytes) present, indicating increased marrow turnover,,. Normal erythrocyte and leukocyte morphology. Excludes pseudothrombocytopenia (platelet clumping),.
- **Coagulation Studies:** Prothrombin time (PT), activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT), and fibrinogen remain normal.
- **Platelet Antibodies:** Testing not routinely recommended due to poor specificity; antibodies absent in up to 40% of cases,.
- **Bone Marrow Examination:**
    - Reveals normal granulopoiesis and erythropoiesis.
    - Normal or increased numbers of megakaryocytes, frequently exhibiting immature morphology,.
    - Not routinely indicated for classic pediatric presentation.
    - Indications for marrow evaluation: Abnormal WBC count/differential, unexplained anemia, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, bone pain, failure to respond to initial intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) therapy, or prior to initiation of corticosteroid therapy (to definitively exclude leukemia),,.

### Advanced Diagnostic Testing (For Persistent/Chronic Cases)

- Direct antiglobulin test (DAT/Coombs) to exclude Evans syndrome (concurrent autoimmune hemolytic anemia),.
- Antinuclear antibody (ANA), double-stranded DNA to exclude systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE),.
- Viral polymerase chain reaction (PCR)/serology: HIV, HCV, EBV, CMV, Parvovirus.
- Quantitative immunoglobulins to exclude common variable immunodeficiency (CVID).
- _Helicobacter pylori_ testing.
- Thyroid function screening (TSH, free T4, antithyroid antibodies).

## Differential Diagnosis

|Disease Category|Specific Pathologies To Exclude|
|:--|:--|
|**Immune Consumption**|Evans syndrome, SLE, neonatal alloimmune thrombocytopenia, drug-induced thrombocytopenia (valproate, phenytoin, heparin), post-transfusion purpura,,,.|
|**Non-Immune Consumption**|Hemolytic-uremic syndrome (HUS), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), Kasabach-Merritt syndrome,,,.|
|**Bone Marrow Failure**|Fanconi anemia, severe aplastic anemia, myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), marrow infiltration (leukemia, neuroblastoma),.|
|**Inherited Platelet Disorders**|Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome (small platelets), Bernard-Soulier syndrome (giant platelets), MYH9-related disease, congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia (CAMT), thrombocytopenia-absent radius (TAR),,,.|
|**Sequestration**|Portal hypertension, Gaucher disease, hypersplenism,.|

## Principles Of Management

- Primary therapeutic goal: Increase platelet count sufficiently to prevent serious hemorrhage (>20,000/μL); normalization of platelet count is not the objective,,.
- Avoid antiplatelet medications (aspirin, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) and restrict competitive contact sports,.
- 70-80% of acute pediatric cases undergo spontaneous remission within 6 months regardless of therapy,.

### Observation (Watch-And-Wait Strategy)

- Recommended for children exhibiting no symptoms or mild bleeding (bruising, petechiae) regardless of absolute platelet count,,.
- Avoids medication toxicity, high healthcare costs, and procedural anxiety,.

### First-Line Pharmacologic Interventions

Reserved for moderate-to-severe mucosal bleeding or profound hemorrhagic risk,.

**1. Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)**

- **Dose:** 0.8-1.0 g/kg/day for 1-2 days,,.
- **Mechanism:** Downregulates Fc-receptor mediated phagocytosis of antibody-coated platelets; saturates FcRn promoting rapid autoantibody catabolism,.
- **Efficacy:** Rapidly increases platelet count (>20,000/μL) within 48 hours in 95% of patients. Preferred over steroids in children <2 years due to lower steroid response rates.
- **Adverse Effects:** Headache, emesis, aseptic meningitis, high medication cost,,,.

**2. Corticosteroids**

- **Dose:** Prednisone 4 mg/kg/day orally for 4-5 days (without taper), or 1-2 mg/kg/day for 2-3 weeks,,,. High-dose dexamethasone or methylprednisolone also utilized.
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits macrophage phagocytosis, suppresses autoantibody production, and dampens T-cell activation.
- **Efficacy:** Effective in 70-80% of patients; slower response compared to IVIG,.
- **Adverse Effects:** Cushingoid facies, weight gain, hypertension, hyperglycemia, mood lability, cataracts, growth retardation (with prolonged use),.

**3. Intravenous Anti-D Immune Globulin**

- **Dose:** 50-75 μg/kg administered as a single dose,.
- **Indications:** Restricted to Rh-positive, direct antiglobulin test (DAT)-negative patients with an intact spleen,.
- **Mechanism:** Induces mild extravascular hemolysis; resulting antibody-coated erythrocytes competitively saturate splenic Fc receptors, sparing platelets.
- **Adverse Effects:** Expected hemoglobin drop of 0.5-2.0 g/dL, fever, chills, headache; rare but severe intravascular hemolysis,.

## Emergency Interventions For Life-Threatening Hemorrhage

- Intracranial hemorrhage or massive internal bleeding dictates immediate, multimodal, aggressive intervention,.
- Do not await response from single-agent therapies.
- **Protocol:**
    - **Platelet Transfusion:** Administer bolus followed by continuous infusion. Normally contraindicated due to rapid autoantibody clearance, but essential for immediate mechanical hemostasis,,,.
    - **Intravenous Methylprednisolone:** 30 mg/kg/day for 1-3 days.
    - **IVIG:** 1 g/kg/day for 2-3 days.
    - **Adjunctive Agents:** Single dose of IV Anti-D (75 μg/kg), Romiplostim (10 μg/kg), or Rituximab (375 mg/m2).
    - **Recombinant Factor VIIa (rFVIIa):** Utilized for bypassing hemostatic defects.
    - **Emergency Splenectomy:** Considered if hemorrhage remains uncontrolled despite maximal medical stabilization,.

## Management Of Chronic And Refractory Disease

- Defined by thrombocytopenia persisting >12 months,.
- Mandates rigorous reevaluation for underlying immunodeficiency or systemic autoimmunity.
- Normalizing platelet counts remains unnecessary; objective is maintaining safe counts preventing hemorrhage.

### Thrombopoietin Receptor Agonists (TPO-RAs)

- First-line medical therapy for chronic pediatric ITP,.
- **Agents:**
    - **Eltrombopag:** Oral administration (25-50 mg/day based on age/weight),.
    - **Romiplostim:** Subcutaneous administration (1-10 μg/kg weekly),.
- **Mechanism:** Directly stimulate megakaryopoiesis and platelet production via JAK2/STAT5 pathway signaling, compensating for peripheral destruction,.
- **Efficacy:** Achieves durable platelet responses (>50,000/μL) in 64-88% of refractory patients,.
- **Adverse Effects:** Headache, upper respiratory tract infections, transaminitis, potential bone marrow reticulin fibrosis. Eltrombopag requires monitoring for hepatotoxicity and cataracts,.

### Rituximab

- Chimeric monoclonal antibody targeting CD20 transmembrane antigen, resulting in profound B-cell depletion.
- **Dose:** 375 mg/m2 intravenously weekly for 4 consecutive weeks.
- **Efficacy:** Initial response rates 40-50%; long-term sustained remission in 25-30% of children. Highest curative potential noted in adolescent females.
- **Adverse Effects:** Infusion reactions (hives, bronchospasm), serum sickness, transient hypogammaglobulinemia, rare reactivation of latent viral infections,.

### Splenectomy

- Eradicates primary site of autoantibody synthesis and platelet destruction.
- **Indications:** Strictly reserved for severe chronic ITP (>1 year duration) unresponsive to medical therapy with severely impaired quality of life, or acute life-threatening hemorrhage refractory to maximal medical therapy,. Extremely rare in children <5 years.
- **Efficacy:** Induces complete remission in 64-88% of pediatric patients,.
- **Complications:** Lifelong susceptibility to overwhelming postsplenectomy infection (OPSI) by encapsulated organisms, increased stroke/thrombosis risk, and subsequent pulmonary hypertension,.
- **Prerequisites:** Mandatory preoperative immunization (pneumococcal, meningococcal, _Haemophilus influenzae_) and lifelong postoperative penicillin prophylaxis,.

### Second-Line Immunosuppressive Therapies

- **Mycophenolate Mofetil / Sirolimus (Rapamycin):** Highly effective for ITP secondary to Evans syndrome or Autoimmune Lymphoproliferative Syndrome (ALPS),.
- **Alternative Agents:** Azathioprine, Danazol (avoid in prepubertal children due to premature epiphyseal closure), Dapsone, Cyclosporine, Cyclophosphamide, and Vincristine demonstrate variable efficacy in highly refractory cases,.
