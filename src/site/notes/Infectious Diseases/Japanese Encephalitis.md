## Introduction And Virology

- Japanese Encephalitis represents a mosquito-borne viral disease causing acute brain inflammation, serving as the leading cause of vaccine-preventable viral encephalitis in Asia.
- The etiologic agent constitutes a single-stranded, positive-sense RNA virus belonging to the _Flavivirus_ genus within the _Flaviviridae_ family.
- The viral envelope glycoprotein functions as the major surface protein, playing a critical role in receptor binding and inducing neutralizing host antibodies.
- While Genotype III historically dominated globally, Genotype I has recently emerged as the dominant circulating strain across Asia.

## Epidemiology And Transmission

- Transmission strictly follows an enzootic cycle involving mosquito vectors and vertebrate hosts.
![](https://www.cdc.gov/japanese-encephalitis/media/images/JETransmissionCycle1200x675.png)

|Transmission Component|Specific Details|
|---|---|
|**Primary Vector**|Transmitted primarily by _Culex tritaeniorhynchus_ mosquitoes, which breed prolifically in stagnant water bodies like rice paddies.|
|**Natural Reservoirs**|Ardeid birds, such as herons and egrets, naturally maintain the virus in the environment.|
|**Amplifying Hosts**|Pigs serve as the most critical amplifying hosts, developing high-titer viremia sufficient to infect feeding mosquitoes.|
|**Dead-End Hosts**|Humans and horses represent incidental, dead-end hosts because they fail to develop sufficient viremia to infect mosquitoes.|
## Pathophysiology

- Following an infected mosquito bite, the virus undergoes local replication and enters regional lymph nodes, triggering a transient viremia.
- Neuroinvasion occurs across the blood-brain barrier via passive transport, infected leukocyte transit, or olfactory neuronal entry.
- The virus causes direct neuronal destruction and provokes an intense inflammatory response featuring perivascular lymphocytic cuffing and microglial nodules.
- Neuropathology characteristically localizes to the thalamus, basal ganglia, and brainstem.

## Clinical Manifestations

- The standard incubation period ranges from 4 to 14 days.
- While over 99% of infections remain completely asymptomatic, clinical illness progresses through three distinct symptomatic stages.

| Clinical Stage               | Duration And Key Features                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prodromal Stage**          | ➤ Lasts 2 to 3 days; presents with abrupt high-grade fever, chills, severe headache, anorexia, and vomiting.                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Acute Encephalitic Stage** | ➤ Lasts 3 to 4 days; features progressive clouding of consciousness, delirium, and coma. <br>➤ Generalized tonic-clonic seizures manifest frequently in pediatric patients. <br>➤ Extrapyramidal signs represent a distinctive hallmark, presenting as mask-like facies, cogwheel rigidity, dystonia, and choreoathetoid movements. |
| **Convalescent Stage**       | ➤ Lasts weeks to months; fever subsides, but persistent neurological sequelae remain highly common.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |

## Diagnostic Evaluation

|Diagnostic Modality|Characteristic Findings|
|---|---|
|**Serology**|Cerebrospinal fluid or serum IgM Capture ELISA represents the gold standard diagnostic method, successfully detecting antibodies within 7 days of symptom onset.|
|**Neuroimaging**|Magnetic Resonance Imaging demonstrates characteristic bilateral hyperintensities on T2 and FLAIR sequences prominently involving the thalamus and basal ganglia.|
|**Cerebrospinal Fluid**|Analysis typically reveals mild to moderate lymphocytic pleocytosis, mildly elevated protein, and strictly normal glucose levels.|

## Management Protocol

- No specific antiviral therapy currently exists; clinical management remains strictly supportive.
- Airway patency must be rigorously secured, occasionally requiring intubation and mechanical ventilation for comatose patients.
- Raised intracranial pressure mandates head elevation to 30 degrees and the administration of hyperosmolar therapy like Mannitol.
- Meticulous fluid monitoring is essential to detect and properly manage concurrent Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone secretion.
- Seizures demand aggressive control utilizing intravenous Benzodiazepines, immediately followed by Phenytoin administration.

## Prognosis And Prevention

- The disease carries a high mortality rate ranging from 20% to 30%, occurring primarily due to raised intracranial pressure or acute respiratory failure.
- Permanent neurological or psychiatric sequelae, including severe motor deficits and intellectual impairment, afflict 30% to 50% of disease survivors.
- Universal vaccination constitutes the single most effective preventive strategy.
- The live attenuated SA 14-14-2 vaccine is routinely administered in two doses at 9 months and 16 to 24 months of age under the Universal Immunization Program in highly endemic districts.
- Environmental control necessitates the strict segregation of pigs from human habitats and rigorous vector control targeting rice paddies.
