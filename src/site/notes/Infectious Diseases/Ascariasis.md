## Introduction And Etiology

- Ascariasis represents a helminthic infection caused by _Ascaris lumbricoides_, the largest nematode parasitizing the human intestine.
- Adult females measure 20 to 35 cm in length, whereas males are smaller at 15 to 30 cm.
- A single gravid female possesses the reproductive potential to produce approximately 200,000 eggs daily.
- Fertilized eggs display an oval shape with a thick, mamillated, bile-stained outer shell and exhibit extreme environmental resistance, remaining viable in soil for 7 to 10 years.

## Epidemiology And Transmission

- Ascariasis constitutes the most prevalent global helminthic infection, affecting up to 1 billion individuals.
- The highest prevalence and infection intensity occur in preschool and early school-age children.
- Transmission strictly follows the fecal-oral route through the ingestion of infective embryonated eggs from contaminated soil, water, or agricultural produce.

## Life Cycle
![Ascariasis LifeCycle 19](https://images.pedianotes.in/2026/06/1cbliz5oy7W8RdRFchlJ)

|Migration Phase|Biological Events|
|---|---|
|**Intestinal Hatching**|Ingested eggs hatch within the duodenum, releasing larvae that actively penetrate the intestinal mucosa.|
|**Hepatic Migration**|Larvae enter the portal circulation and are transported directly to the liver.|
|**Pulmonary Migration**|Larvae travel via the systemic circulation to the lungs, breaking out of capillaries into the alveolar spaces.|
|**Glottis Transit**|Larvae migrate upward through the bronchi and trachea, pass over the epiglottis, and are swallowed.|
|**Intestinal Maturation**|Larvae return to the small intestine, undergoing final molts to mature into adult worms over 8 to 10 weeks.|

## Pathophysiology

### Larval Phase (Migratory)

- Migrating larvae provoke a prominent host inflammatory response characterized by intense eosinophilic infiltration.
- Rupture through alveolar walls induces minute hemorrhages and pneumonitis.
- Larval antigens induce a Th2 immune response featuring high IgE production, which serves as an immunomodulatory mechanism linked to asthma.

### Adult Phase (Intestinal)

- Adult worms actively compete with the host for nutrients and secrete specific peptides that inhibit pancreatic enzymes like trypsin and chymotrypsin.
- This enzymatic inhibition directly causes malabsorption of proteins, contributing to malnutrition, lactose intolerance, and permanent growth stunting.
- Large worm aggregates exert mechanical effects, leading to intestinal obstruction, volvulus, or intussusception.
- Motile adult worms exhibit aberrant migration into the biliary tree, pancreatic duct, or appendix, typically triggered by fever or anesthesia.

## Clinical Manifestations

|Disease Syndrome|Clinical Findings|
|---|---|
|**Pulmonary Ascariasis**|Termed Loeffler's Syndrome; presents 4 to 16 days post-infection with cough, dyspnea, wheezing, and fever.|
|**Intestinal Ascariasis**|Frequently presents with vague abdominal discomfort, nausea, anorexia, and vomiting.|
|**Intestinal Obstruction**|A bolus of tangled worms blocks the terminal ileum or ileocecal valve, causing bilious vomiting, distension, and obstipation.|
|**Hepatobiliary Disease**|Migration across the ampulla of Vater induces biliary colic, ascending cholangitis, acalculous cholecystitis, or acute pancreatitis.|
|**Malnutrition**|Chronic heavy infections precipitate failure to thrive, hypoalbuminemia, and vitamin A deficiency.|

## Diagnostic Evaluation

|Diagnostic Modality|Key Findings|
|---|---|
|**Stool Microscopy**|Serves as the gold standard; direct saline smears detect characteristic mamillated eggs.|
|**Hematology**|Significant peripheral eosinophilia manifests prominently during the pulmonary larval migration phase.|
|**Ultrasonography**|Highly sensitive for detecting adult worms in the biliary tree as linear echogenic structures, or revealing a bull's eye appearance in the intestine.|
|**Radiography**|Abdominal X-rays reveal a whirlpool sign of worm masses; Chest X-rays demonstrate transient, shifting pulmonary infiltrates.|

## Management

### Pharmacological Therapy

- Albendazole serves as the definitive drug of choice, administered as a 400 mg single oral dose for patients older than 2 years, and 200 mg for children aged 12 to 24 months.
- Mebendazole represents an effective alternative, prescribed as 100 mg twice daily for 3 days or as a 500 mg single dose.
- Pyrantel Pamoate or Ivermectin provide additional therapeutic options.
- Anthelmintic agents lack efficacy against migratory larvae; therefore, pulmonary ascariasis strictly requires supportive care utilizing bronchodilators and corticosteroids until worms mature in the intestine.

### Management Of Complications

- Intestinal obstruction initially mandates conservative management encompassing intravenous fluids, nasogastric suction, and rigorous electrolyte correction.
- Piperazine Citrate induces flaccid paralysis of the worms, facilitating expulsion without inciting agitation during obstruction.
- Surgical intervention remains strictly indicated for complete obstruction, volvulus, perforation, or ischemia.
- Biliary ascariasis typically resolves with conservative antispasmodic therapy, though Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography is utilized if mechanical extraction proves necessary.

## Prevention And Control

- Primary prevention necessitates the safe sanitary disposal of human excreta and rigorous hand hygiene.
- Mass Drug Administration programs advocate periodic deworming using Albendazole for children residing in endemic communities exhibiting a prevalence exceeding 20%.
