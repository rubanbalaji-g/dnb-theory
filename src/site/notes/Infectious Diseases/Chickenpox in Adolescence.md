## Introduction And Epidemiology

- Chickenpox is a primary infection caused by the Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV), a neurotropic alpha-herpesvirus.
- While classically a benign childhood disease, it presents with significantly higher morbidity, mortality, and complication rates when it occurs in adolescents, defined roughly as 10 to 19 years of age.
- In tropical and subtropical regions, the peak age of infection is frequently delayed, resulting in a higher proportion of susceptible adolescents who are at greater risk for severe disease.

## Pathophysiology And Transmission

- VZV is highly contagious and spreads primarily via aerosolized respiratory droplets or through direct contact with vesicular fluid.
- Patients remain infectious from 24 to 48 hours prior to rash onset until all skin lesions have completely crusted over, typically 4 to 7 days after the rash appears.
- Following inhalation, the virus replicates in the upper respiratory tract and regional lymph nodes, constituting the primary viremia.
- Subsequent secondary viremia disseminates the virus to the reticuloendothelial system, skin, and viscera.
- The standard incubation period ranges from 10 to 21 days, averaging 14 to 16 days.

## Clinical Manifestations

### Prodromal Phase

- Adolescents frequently experience a distinct and severe prodrome lasting 24 to 48 hours prior to the appearance of the exanthem.
- Symptoms include high fever reaching up to 41°C, profound malaise, anorexia, headache, and mild abdominal pain.
- These systemic symptoms often persist for 2 to 4 days after the onset of the rash.

### Exanthematous Phase

- The rash begins as intensely pruritic erythematous macules that rapidly evolve into papules, followed by clear, fluid-filled vesicles classically described as a <span style="color:rgb(255, 87, 87)">dewdrop on a rose petal</span>.
- Vesicles become cloudy and pustular within 24 to 48 hours, eventually umbilicating and crusting.
- The distribution is characteristically centripetal, originating on the scalp, face, and trunk before spreading to the extremities.
- A hallmark feature is cropping, defined as the simultaneous presence of lesions in all stages of evolution within the same anatomical area.
- Adolescents consistently develop a higher burden of lesions, frequently exceeding 500, with new crops continuing to appear for more than 7 days.

## Complications

Adolescents face a markedly increased risk of severe complications compared to young children.

|Complication Category|Clinical Features|
|---|---|
|**Varicella Pneumonia**|The most serious complication in healthy adolescents, presenting 1 to 6 days post-rash with cough, dyspnea, tachypnea, and pleuritic chest pain; radiography reveals diffuse bilateral infiltrates.|
|**Secondary Bacterial Infection**|Superinfection of skin lesions by _Staphylococcus aureus_ or Group A _Streptococcus_, manifesting as impetigo, cellulitis, or severe necrotizing fasciitis.|
|**Neurological**|Cerebellar ataxia presents with self-limiting gait disturbance and nystagmus; encephalitis presents with altered sensorium and seizures, carrying higher morbidity.|
|**Hematologic And Systemic**|Mild thrombocytopenia is common; rare complications include purpura fulminans, hepatitis, myocarditis, and nephritis.|

## Diagnostic Evaluation

- Diagnosis remains primarily clinical, relying on the characteristic pleomorphic rash and exposure history.
- Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) serves as the most sensitive diagnostic method, detecting VZV DNA in vesicular fluid or crusts.
- Direct Fluorescence Assay provides rapid and specific detection utilizing cellular material from the vesicle base.
- Tzanck smears reveal multinucleated giant cells but cannot distinguish VZV from Herpes Simplex Virus.

## Management Protocol

### Pharmacological Therapy

- Antiviral therapy is universally recommended for all unvaccinated adolescents over 12 years of age due to the elevated risk of severe disease.
- Therapy must be initiated within 24 hours of exanthem onset to ensure clinical efficacy.

|Regimen Type|Pharmacological Guidelines|
|---|---|
|**Oral First-Line**|Acyclovir administered at 800 mg five times daily for 5 to 7 days, or Valacyclovir administered at 1000 mg three times daily for 5 days.|
|**Intravenous Therapy**|Intravenous Acyclovir administered at 10 mg/kg every 8 hours for 7 to 10 days; strictly reserved for severe complications like pneumonia or encephalitis.|

### Supportive Care

- Administer Paracetamol for fever control; Aspirin is strictly contraindicated due to the high risk of Reye syndrome.
- Ibuprofen is generally avoided due to a theoretical risk of exacerbating severe streptococcal skin infections.
- Utilize oral antihistamines and soothing lotions like calamine to manage intense pruritus.
- Patients must be strictly isolated from school and public settings until every lesion has crusted over.

## Prevention And Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

- Unvaccinated adolescents strictly require a catch-up vaccination series consisting of two doses of the live-attenuated varicella vaccine separated by 4 to 8 weeks.
- Following a susceptible exposure, administering the vaccine within 3 to 5 days provides effective post-exposure prophylaxis.
- Varicella-Zoster Immune Globulin is reserved for high-risk exposed individuals who possess contraindications to the live vaccine.
