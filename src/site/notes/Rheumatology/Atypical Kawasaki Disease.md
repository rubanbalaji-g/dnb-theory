## Introduction And Epidemiology

- Kawasaki Disease with Atypical Features
- Features prolonged unexplained fever.
- Manifests fewer than four principal clinical features.
- Carries profoundly high risk for coronary artery abnormalities.
- Diagnosed via compatible laboratory or echocardiographic findings.
- Occurs most frequently in young infants under six months of age.
- Delayed diagnosis common due to incomplete clinical picture.
- Clinical features may evolute sequentially over time.

## Etiology And Pathophysiology

- Exact etiology remains unknown.
- Presumed infectious trigger in genetically susceptible individuals.
- Vascular injury progresses through three distinct histological phases.
- Initial phase involves neutrophilic necrotizing arteritis occurring in first two weeks.
- Destroys internal elastic lamina, yielding saccular aneurysms.
- Second phase involves subacute vasculitis driven by lymphocytes and eosinophils.
- Final phase involves smooth muscle myofibroblast proliferation.
- Causes progressive luminal stenosis and occlusive thrombosis.

## Clinical Manifestations And Atypical Features

- High-grade, unremitting fever exceeding five days serves as primary hallmark.
- Extreme irritability prominent in infants, likely secondary to aseptic meningitis.
- Classic mucocutaneous features often absent or fleeting.

|Organ System|Atypical Or Uncommon Clinical Features|
|:--|:--|
|Lymphatic|Node-first presentation. Unilateral tender cervical lymphadenopathy. Ultrasonography reveals cluster of grapes appearance. Nodes uniformly hypoechoic with well-circumscribed margins, lacking necrosis.|
|Genitourinary|Acute hydrocele. Sterile pyuria, meatitis, dysuria. Acute renal failure, tubulointerstitial nephritis.|
|Gastrointestinal|Severe abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea. Hydrops of gallbladder presenting as upper abdominal mass. Mild hepatitis, transiently elevated transaminases.|
|Neurological|Aseptic meningitis yielding cerebrospinal fluid pleocytosis. Transient sensorineural hearing loss affecting high frequency zones. Acute hemiplegia, facial nerve palsy.|
|Dermatological|Erythema and induration at bacillus Calmette-Guerin inoculation site. Perianal erythema with desquamation.|

## Diagnostic Evaluation And Biomarkers

- Demands extremely high index of suspicion.
- Any infant under six months with unexplained fever exceeding seven days mandates immediate echocardiography.
- Elevated pro-brain natriuretic peptide exceeding 500 pg/mL strongly supports diagnosis in ambiguous cases.
- Elevated gamma-glutamyl transferase without frank liver dysfunction provides significant diagnostic clue.
- Diagnosis established utilizing standardized diagnostic algorithms integrating laboratory and echocardiographic parameters.

### Laboratory Diagnostic Criteria

- Evaluation indicated when C-reactive protein equals or exceeds 3.0 mg/dL, OR erythrocyte sedimentation rate equals or exceeds 40 mm/hr.
- Diagnosis confirmed if three or more supplemental laboratory criteria exist.

|Laboratory Marker|Diagnostic Threshold For Incomplete Disease|
|:--|:--|
|Serum Albumin|Less than or equal to 3.0 g/dL.|
|Hemoglobin|Anemia for age.|
|Hepatic Enzymes|Elevation of alanine aminotransferase.|
|Platelet Count|Greater than or equal to 450,000/mm3 after seventh day of fever.|
|Leukocyte Count|Greater than or equal to 15,000/mm3.|
|Urinalysis|Greater than or equal to 10 white blood cells per high-power field.|

### Echocardiographic Diagnostic Criteria

- Echocardiogram considered positive for incomplete disease if specific structural or functional conditions met.

|Echocardiographic Finding|Specific Diagnostic Conditions|
|:--|:--|
|Direct Aneurysm|Coronary artery aneurysm observed directly.|
|Coronary Dilation|Z score of left anterior descending or right coronary artery greater than or equal to 2.5.|
|Suggestive Features|Presence of three or more subtle criteria establishes positive scan. Includes perivascular brightness, lack of vessel tapering. Includes decreased left ventricular function, mitral regurgitation, pericardial effusion. Includes Z scores in left anterior descending or right coronary artery between 2.0 and 2.5.|

## Differential Diagnosis

- Presentation mimics multiple infectious and inflammatory syndromes.

|Disease Category|Distinguishing Clinical And Laboratory Features|
|:--|:--|
|Bacterial Infections|Scarlet fever demonstrates rapid response to antibiotics, elevated anti-streptolysin O titers. Staphylococcal toxic shock syndrome features renal insufficiency, pancytopenia, severe coagulopathy. Leptospirosis involves severe myalgias, renal failure, hepatic failure.|
|Viral Infections|Adenovirus features prominent exudative pharyngitis and exudative conjunctivitis. Measles features Koplik spots, leukopenia, characteristic descending rash. Epstein-Barr virus demonstrates generalized lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly.|
|Rheumatologic Diseases|Systemic-onset juvenile idiopathic arthritis features quotidian fever pattern, prominent hepatosplenomegaly. Polyarteritis nodosa features necrotizing vasculitis, severe renal hypertension.|
|Emerging Syndromes|Multisystem inflammatory syndrome in children affects older demographics. Features profound gastrointestinal pain, severe myocardial dysfunction, profound lymphopenia, marked hyperferritinemia.|

## Management Strategies

- Requires immediate therapeutic intervention to prevent irreversible coronary architecture damage.
- Standard primary therapy incorporates intravenous immunoglobulin and aspirin.
- Intravenous immunoglobulin 2 g/kg administered as single infusion over 8 to 12 hours.
- Acute phase aspirin dosing restricted to 3 to 5 mg/kg/day to minimize gastrointestinal bleeding risks.
- Antiplatelet aspirin therapy continued minimum 6 to 8 weeks.
- Aspirin discontinued entirely if serial echocardiograms remain completely normal.
- Live viral vaccines deferred for 11 months following intravenous immunoglobulin administration.

### Intensification Therapy

- High-risk patients mandate immediate intensification of primary regimen.
- Infants under six months uniformly require intensification due to exceptional aneurysm risk.
- Baseline coronary artery Z score exceeding 2.5 mandates intensification.
- Pharmacologic options include intravenous methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg/day.
- Infliximab 10 mg/kg infused over 2 hours serves as potent biological alternative.
- Statins (75 mg/kg/day) modulate endothelial function and reduce oxidative stress in persistent disease.

### Management Of Refractory Disease

- Defined as persistent or recrudescent fever 36 hours post-immunoglobulin infusion.
- Necessitates prompt administration of second intravenous immunoglobulin dose.
- Pulse intravenous methylprednisolone 30 mg/kg/day utilized for severe resistance.
- Refractory cases warrant Cyclosporine A or Anakinra administration.

## Prognosis And Complications

- Delayed diagnosis independently drives highest rates of coronary artery aneurysms.
- Giant aneurysms (Z score greater than or equal to 10) carry lifetime risk of occlusive thrombosis, myocardial infarction, and sudden death.
- Giant aneurysms necessitate lifelong dual antiplatelet therapy coupled with systemic anticoagulation (Low molecular weight heparin or direct oral anticoagulants).
- Mandatory long-term surveillance incorporates advanced imaging modalities including computed tomography angiography or magnetic resonance imaging.
- Aneurysms may regress, however regressed vessels maintain permanent myointimal thickening and abnormal vascular reactivity.
