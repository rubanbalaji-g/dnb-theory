## Definition
- Child abuse or child maltreatment encompasses all forms of physical and/or emotional ill-treatment, sexual abuse, neglect or negligent treatment, or commercial or other exploitation, resulting in actual or potential harm to the child’s health, survival, development, or dignity in the context of a relationship of responsibility, trust, or power. 
- In India, the legal framework is primarily governed by the **Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015** (handling cruelty and neglect) and the **Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012** (handling sexual abuse).
## Classification and Pathophysiology
Maltreatment is categorized into four primary types, often co-existing in a single child:
### 1. Physical Abuse
- **Mechanism:** Non-accidental physical injury inflicted by a parent, caregiver, or guardian.
- **Pathophysiology:** Direct mechanical trauma (fractures, visceral injury) or acceleration-deceleration forces causing rotational shearing of subdural vessels and diffuse axonal injury, classically seen in Abusive Head Trauma (AHT) or Shaken Baby Syndrome.
### 2. Sexual Abuse
- Involvement of a child in sexual activities that they cannot give informed consent to, do not fully comprehend, or violate developmental boundaries.
### 3. Emotional / Psychological Abuse
- Chronic patterns of failure to provide a supportive environment (e.g., berating, terrorizing, isolating, or exploiting), severely disrupting the development of the central nervous system architecture via toxic stress and chronic hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis activation.
### 4. Neglect
- Failure to provide for a child's basic physical, medical, educational, or emotional needs despite having the resources to do so. It is the most common form of maltreatment globally and leads to severe non-organic failure to thrive (NOFTT).
## Clinical Features and Red Flags
A pediatrician must maintain a high index of suspicion. Key indicators include:
### 1. History Indicators
- Discrepancy between the history provided and the clinical severity of the injury.
- Vague, changing, or absent explanations for significant trauma.
- Delay in seeking medical attention for acute, severe injuries.
- History of injury attributed to a developmentally impossible task (e.g., a 2-month-old rolling off a bed).
### 2. Physical Abuse Signposts
- **Cutaneous Lesions:** Bruises on protected or unusual areas (ears, neck, cheeks, torso, buttocks, inner thighs). Bruises of varying colors indicating different stages of healing. Patterned marks resembling objects (belts, cords, handprints).
- **Burns:** Immersion burns with sharp demarcation lines ("glove and stocking" pattern) and sparing of flexor creases (donut sparing), or circular cigarette burns.
- **Abusive Head Trauma (AHT):** Irritability, lethargy, seizures, bulging fontanelle, vomiting without diarrhea, and the classic triad: subdural hematoma, diffuse brain injury, and retinal hemorrhages (often bilateral and multilayered).
### 3. Neglect Signposts
- Severe wasting, poor hygiene, severe unmanaged diaper dermatitis, untreated chronic medical conditions, or failure to meet immunization milestones.
## Medical Evaluation and Investigations

Evaluation requires a dual focus: diagnosing acute pathology and systematically documenting forensic evidence.
### 1. Radiological Evaluation
- **Skeletal Survey:** Mandatory in all children under 2 years with suspected physical abuse. Includes AP and lateral views of the skull, long bones, spine, ribs, hands, and feet.
- **High-Specificity Fractures:** Posterior rib fractures (from compressive squeezing), metaphyseal corner fractures or "bucket-handle" fractures (from pulling/twisting limbs), scapular, sternal, or spinous process fractures.
- **Neuroimaging:** Non-contrast CT head for acute intracranial injury (subdural or epidural hematoma). Brain MRI for subacute or chronic changes, parenchymal contusions, and axonal shear injuries.
### 2. Laboratory Investigations
- **Coagulation Profile:** PT, APTT, INR, CBC with platelets, and bleeding time to rule out bleeding diatheses in children presenting with extensive bruising or intracranial hemorrhage.
- **Metabolic Bone Workup:** Serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, parathyroid hormone, and Vitamin D levels to rule out osteogenesis imperfecta, rickets, or metabolic bone disease.
- **Visceral Trauma Screen:** Serum amylase, lipase, liver function tests (AST, ALT), and urinalysis (for hematuria) to evaluate for occult abdominal trauma.

### 3. Ophthalmological Evaluation
- Dilated fundoscopic examination by an ophthalmologist within 24 hours of suspected AHT to look for retinal hemorrhages.
## Management Protocols

### 1. Acute Medical Stabilization
- Admit the child immediately if safety is compromised, regardless of injury severity.
- Standard trauma resuscitation protocols (Airway, Breathing, Circulation), neuro-intensive management for elevated intracranial pressure, and surgical intervention for internal injuries or complex fractures.
### 2. Multidisciplinary Safeguarding and Rehabilitation
- **Psychological Support:** Trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT) for the child and supportive counseling for non-offending caregivers.
- **Nutritional Rehabilitation:** Structured, high-calorie refeeding protocol for severe neglect and non-organic failure to thrive.
