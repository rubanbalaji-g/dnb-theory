## Introduction And Disease Burden

- Congenital Cytomegalovirus (cCMV) affects 0.5% to 0.7% of all live births globally.
- It is the leading non-genetic infectious cause of sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL) and neurodevelopmental disabilities, including cerebral palsy and cognitive impairments.
- Only 10% to 15% of infected neonates present as symptomatic at birth with microcephaly, petechiae, hepatosplenomegaly, and intrauterine growth restriction.
- Crucially, up to 15% to 20% of asymptomatic infants develop late-onset, progressive, or fluctuating SNHL well beyond the neonatal period.

## The Paradigm Shift: Targeted Vs. Universal Screening

- Targeted screening (testing only those who fail newborn hearing screens or show clinical signs) misses 43% to 50% of infected neonates who eventually develop SNHL.
- Diagnosis requires direct viral identification within the first 21 days of life to differentiate true congenital infection from postnatally acquired infection.
- The 2026 "Minnesota Experience" proved universal screening effectively lowered the average age of the first audiology visit from 8.5 months to roughly 25 days and drastically increased case detection rates.

## Diagnostic Modalities

|Modality|Clinical Utility And Protocol|
|:--|:--|
|**Saliva PCR**|Preferred screening tool with >95% sensitivity. Must be collected 1 to 2 hours after breastfeeding to avoid false positives from maternal CMV shed in breastmilk.|
|**Urine PCR**|The gold standard confirmatory test. Mandatory to confirm any positive saliva screen within the 21-day diagnostic window.|
|**Dried Blood Spot (DBS) PCR**|Leverages existing newborn metabolic screening cards. Advanced high-throughput kits now offer excellent analytical sensitivity, making nationwide universal screening logistically feasible.|

## Clinical Evaluation Protocol For Positive Screens

|Domain|Required Investigations|
|:--|:--|
|**Laboratory**|Complete blood count (to detect thrombocytopenia/neutropenia), metabolic panel, and liver function tests (specifically for elevated ALT and conjugated hyperbilirubinemia).|
|**Neuroimaging**|Cranial ultrasound is the first line to check for periventricular calcifications or subependymal cysts. Brain MRI is indicated if ultrasound is abnormal or severe neurological symptoms exist.|
|**Audiology**|Comprehensive baseline Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR) to detect immediate SNHL.|
|**Ophthalmology**|Dilated fundoscopic examination to rule out chorioretinitis, optic atrophy, or cataracts.|

## Pharmacological Treatment And Follow-up

- **Symptomatic Infants:** Standard care for symptomatic cCMV with central nervous system involvement is oral Valganciclovir (16 mg/kg/dose twice daily) for a prolonged 6-month duration. IV Ganciclovir is reserved for acutely ill infants or those with severe enteropathy.
- **Asymptomatic Infants:** Treating asymptomatic infants or those with isolated SNHL remains controversial due to severe drug toxicities (dose-limiting neutropenia and hepatotoxicity).
- **Toxicity Monitoring:** Requires weekly complete blood counts and liver function tests for the first 4 weeks, followed by monthly checks for the 6-month treatment duration.
- **Audiological Surveillance:** Mandatory hearing tests every 3 to 6 months for the first 3 years of life, then annually until 6 years of age to catch late-onset SNHL.

## Indian Context And Implementation Challenges

- Seroprevalence among Indian women of childbearing age often exceeds 90%, meaning non-primary infections (reinfection or reactivation) account for the vast majority of cCMV cases.
- To overcome the economic and logistical barriers of commercial PCR kits, Indian initiatives are actively exploring multiplexing CMV PCR onto existing government-mandated dried blood spot cards used for congenital hypothyroidism screening.
