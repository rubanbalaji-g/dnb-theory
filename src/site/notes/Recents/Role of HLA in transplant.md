## Introduction and Genetic Basis

Human Leukocyte Antigens (HLA) are cell-surface glycoproteins encoded by the Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) gene cluster located on the short arm of chromosome $6$ ($6\text{p}21.3$). HLA molecules exhibit extreme polymorphism and play a fundamental role in transplant immunology, acting as the primary alloantigens that trigger host immune responses.

## Classification of HLA Molecules

HLA molecules are divided into two primary functional classes:

- **Class I (**$\text{HLA-A}$**,** $\text{HLA-B}$**,** $\text{HLA-C}$**)**:
    - **Expression**: Present on all nucleated cells and platelets.
    - **Function**: Present endogenous antigens to $\text{CD8}^+$ cytotoxic T-lymphocytes.
    - **Structure**: Heterodimer composed of an $\alpha$ heavy chain (polymorphic) non-covalently associated with $\beta_2$-microglobulin (encoded on chromosome $15$).
- **Class II (**$\text{HLA-DR}$**,** $\text{HLA-DQ}$**,** $\text{HLA-DP}$**)**:
    - **Expression**: Restituted to antigen-presenting cells (APCs) such as dendritic cells, macrophages, and B-cells (can be induced on vascular endothelial cells during inflammation).
    - **Function**: Present exogenous antigens to $\text{CD4}^+$ helper T-lymphocytes
    - **Structure**: Heterodimer of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ chains (both polymorphic).

## Mechanisms of HLA-Mediated Allorecognition

When a graft is transplanted, the recipient's T-cells recognize foreign HLA antigens via two primary pathways:

- **Direct Pathway**: Recipient T-cells directly recognize intact, foreign HLA molecules expressed on donor-derived APCs (passenger leukocytes) within the graft. This pathway is highly potent and drives acute cellular rejection.
- **Indirect Pathway**: Recipient APCs process shed donor HLA proteins and present donor-derived HLA peptide fragments on self-MHC molecules to recipient T-cells. This pathway plays a dominant role in chronic rejection and antibody-mediated rejection (AMR).

## Role of HLA Matching in Different Transplants

HLA compatibility requirements vary significantly depending on the organ or tissue transplanted:

### 1. Solid Organ Transplantation (e.g., Kidney)

- **Target Matches**: Primarily focuses on matching $\text{HLA-A}$, $\text{HLA-B}$, and $\text{HLA-DR}$ (often referred to as a "6-antigen match").
- **Impact**: Minimizing HLA mismatches directly correlates with reduced rates of acute rejection, improved long-term graft survival, and decreased requirement for high-dose maintenance immunosuppression.
- **Sensitization**: Mismatches predispose the recipient to developing anti-donor HLA antibodies, making re-transplantation extremely difficult.

### 2. Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT)

- **Target Matches**: Demands high-resolution (allele-level) molecular matching.
- **High-Resolution Match**: Standard of care requires an $8/8$ match ($\text{HLA-A, -B, -C, -DRB1}$) or $10/10$ match (including $\text{HLA-DQB1}$).
- **Clinical Implications**:
    - **Graft-versus-Host Disease (GvHD)**: Mediated by donor T-cells recognizing host HLA mismatches. Mismatches at $\text{HLA-C}$ and $\text{HLA-DP}$ are strongly linked to acute GvHD.
    - **Graft Rejection**: Host T-cells/NK-cells rejecting donor stem cells.
    - **Graft-versus-Leukemia (GvL) Effect**: Controlled HLA mismatches can sometimes be exploited to enhance GvL while managing GvHD.

## Immunology Testing and Crossmatching

To ensure histocompatibility, pediatric transplant programs utilize a sequential testing algorithm:

### 1. HLA Typing

- **Serological Typing (Historical)**: Uses microlymphocytotoxicity assays (limited resolution).
- **Molecular Typing (Current Standard)**:
    - **PCR-SSP** (Sequence-Specific Primers) and **PCR-SSOP** (Sequence-Specific Oligonucleotide Probes) for intermediate-to-high resolution.
    - **Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS)**: Provides definitive, allele-level resolution essential for HSCT.        

### 2. Antibody Screening

- **Panel Reactive Antibody (PRA)**: Estimates the percentage of the general population to which a candidate is sensitized.
- **Single Antigen Bead (SAB) Assay (Luminex)**: High-sensitivity assay detecting circulating Donor-Specific Antibodies (DSAs) against specific HLA alleles.

### 3. Crossmatching

Determines the presence of pre-existing recipient antibodies against donor cells:
- **Complement-Dependent Cytotoxicity (CDC) Crossmatch**: Detects complement-fixing IgG/IgM antibodies. A positive CDC crossmatch against donor T-cells is a contraindication to transplantation (predicts hyperacute rejection).
- **Flow Cytometry Crossmatch (FCXM)**: More sensitive than CDC; detects non-complement-fixing low-titer antibodies.
- **Virtual Crossmatch**: An informatics-based approach comparing donor HLA typing with the recipient’s Luminex-defined antibody profile to predict physical crossmatch outcomes.

## Clinical Relevance in Pediatric Transplantation

- **Lifelong Immunosuppression Avoidance**: Maximizing HLA matching is vital in pediatric recipients to lower cumulative immunosuppressive drug toxicity (renal impairment, growth failure, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder - PTLD).
- **Highly Sensitized Pediatric Patients**: Pediatric patients requiring multiple surgeries or blood transfusions prior to transplantation develop high PRAs. They benefit from desensitization protocols (plasmapheresis, IVIG, Rituximab) and paired donor exchange programs.
