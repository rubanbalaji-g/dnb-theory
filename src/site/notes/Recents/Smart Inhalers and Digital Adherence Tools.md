## Introduction and Clinical Rationale

- Poor medication adherence and incorrect inhaler technique represent significant hurdles, affecting 30 to 70% of pediatric asthma patients globally and in India.
- Uncontrolled asthma due to non-adherence leads to frequent exacerbations, school absenteeism, hospitalizations, and oral corticosteroid overuse.
- Traditional monitoring methods, such as self-reporting and pharmacy refill checks, overestimate actual adherence by 20 to 40%.
- Electronic monitoring devices bridge this objective data gap, preventing delayed interventions and unnecessary medication escalation.

## Device Typology and Core Features

- The technology encompasses sensor-equipped metered-dose inhalers (MDIs), dry-powder inhalers (DPIs) with Bluetooth connectivity (e.g., Propeller Health, Adherium SmartTrack), smart spacers, and smart nebulizers.

|Technological Feature|Clinical Utility|
|:--|:--|
|**Audio-visual reminders**|Directly improves routine controller medication compliance through alerts.|
|**Flow sensors**|Measures inhalation flow and volume while providing real-time technique coaching.|
|**Geofencing capabilities**|Identifies specific environmental triggers through location tracking.|
|**Clinician dashboards**|Facilitates shared decision-making and early detection of rescue medication overuse, which predicts impending exacerbations.|

## Latest Research and Evidence (2024–2026)

- A 2025 comprehensive review demonstrated that audio-visual reminder-enabled smart inhalers boosted preventer adherence to 84% compared to 30% in post-emergency department pediatric cohorts.
- Real-world studies from 2025 to 2026 show sustained asthma control, 39% fewer rescue medication uses, and significantly reduced hospitalizations.
- Global meta-analyses link digital tools to a 10 to 15% additional adherence gain when combined with clinician feedback, resulting in measured improvements in FEV1 and quality of life.
- Pediatric safety data is favorable, though device durability is an issue, with breakage rates reaching 37 to 50% in some feedback-enabled trials.

## Society and Government Guidelines

### GINA and IAP Recommendations

- GINA 2025 strongly emphasizes objective adherence assessment as a core component of evaluating uncontrolled asthma.
- Digital tools are recommended as adjuncts to guide step-down therapy in SMART (single maintenance and reliever therapy) protocols and must be utilized to reassess adherence before escalating a child to biologics.
- The Indian Academy of Pediatrics endorses digital tools for refractory cases, technique training, and advocates for their integration into school health programs.

### Indian Implementation Frameworks

- The Ministry of Health and Family Welfare acknowledges these tools under the National Digital Health Mission.
- While not currently procured under public programs, adherence data integration into platforms like U-WIN and IHIP is anticipated to support pollution-linked respiratory surveillance.

## Role of the Pediatrician

- Pediatricians must assess baseline adherence, select age-appropriate connected devices (generally feasible for children >4 to 5 years), and integrate clinical dashboards for virtual follow-up.
- They play a crucial role in counseling parents on early alert benefits and using device data for shared decisions regarding therapy escalation.

## Challenges and Future Directions

- High device costs (₹5,000 to 20,000 per device), the rural-urban digital divide, and data privacy concerns limit equitable access in India.
- Future directions point toward affordable indigenous sensors, artificial intelligence predictive analytics, and integration with wearables for real-time wheeze detection.
