## Introduction And Rationale

- The National Policy for Rare Diseases (NPRD), originally notified in 2021, underwent significant operational enhancements and funding upgrades in 2025.
- Rare diseases affect an estimated 70 to 100 million individuals in India.
- Over 70% of the approximately 450 identified rare disorders are genetic and manifest primarily during childhood.
- Prominent pediatric conditions include lysosomal storage disorders (LSDs), spinal muscular atrophy (SMA), mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS), Gaucher disease, and congenital adrenal hyperplasia.
- The policy aims to mitigate severe diagnostic odysseys (often lasting 5 to 7 years) and exorbitant treatment costs that range from ₹10 lakh to over ₹1 crore annually.

## Disease Categorization Under NPRD

- The policy rationalizes medical care by classifying rare diseases into three distinct groups for targeted financial support.

|Category|Description|Clinical Examples|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Group 1**|Disorders amenable to one-time curative treatment.|Certain lysosomal storage disorders (LSDs).|
|**Group 2**|Diseases requiring long-term or lifelong low-cost manageable treatment.|Specific metabolic or dietary disorders.|
|**Group 3**|Disorders requiring high-cost, lifelong targeted therapies.|Spinal muscular atrophy, Pompe disease, Mucopolysaccharidoses.|

## Key 2025 Policy Updates And Operational Framework

### Financial Support Enhancements

- The 2025 updates officially increased the lifetime financial support to ₹50 lakh per patient.
- This funding is provided under the Rashtriya Arogya Nidhi (RAN) and is applicable across all three disease groups for the 63 formally notified rare diseases.
- A digital crowdfunding portal remains operational to supplement government aid through voluntary contributions.

### Infrastructure And Surveillance

- Centres of Excellence (CoEs) have been strategically expanded to over 15 tertiary institutes nationwide, including the 2025 addition of AIIMS Patna.
- The Indian Council of Medical Research (ICMR) maintains a national rare disease registry, which recorded nearly 1,800 benefiting patients by mid-2026.

## Pediatric Integration And IAP Guidelines

- The Indian Academy of Pediatrics (IAP) strongly endorses the prompt referral of suspected cases to designated CoEs.
- IAP guidelines emphasize the critical need for early genetic testing (utilizing Next-Generation Sequencing panels), multidisciplinary care, and comprehensive family counseling.
- Management frameworks are increasingly integrated with the Rashtriya Bal Swasthya Karyakram (RBSK) and newborn screening programs to facilitate early intervention before irreversible organ damage occurs.

## Pediatrician's Role In Clinical Practice

- Pediatricians serve as the pivotal first point of contact to suspect rare diseases via clinical red flags, such as unexplained developmental delay, organomegaly, or recurrent infections.
- Clinicians must order targeted genetic testing and promptly refer confirmed or highly suspected cases to CoEs for funding approval.
- Counseling families on NPRD eligibility, navigating the crowdfunding portal, and providing meticulous supportive care are essential duties.

## Current Challenges And Future Directions

- Significant gaps persist regarding the exclusion of unlisted diseases and occasional funding denials.
- The ₹50 lakh financial cap remains inadequate for lifelong Group 3 therapies.
- Legal and subjudice matters remain pending before the Supreme Court regarding the creation of a dedicated National Rare Diseases Fund.
