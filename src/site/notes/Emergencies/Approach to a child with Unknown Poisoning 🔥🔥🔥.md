## Initial Stabilization and Resuscitation

- The paramount priority in a child presenting with unknown poisoning is emergency cardiorespiratory stabilization, which must always precede comprehensive diagnostic testing.
- A rapid and systematic assessment of the airway, breathing, circulation, and neurological disability (ABCD) is required immediately upon presentation.
- Early elective endotracheal intubation should be anticipated and performed in patients with profound central nervous system (CNS) depression, absent protective airway reflexes, or acute respiratory failure to mitigate the risk of pulmonary aspiration.
- Hypotension must be aggressively managed initially with rapid intravenous boluses of isotonic crystalloids, such as normal saline or lactated Ringer's.
- If hypotension remains refractory to volume expansion, direct-acting vasopressors (e.g., epinephrine or norepinephrine) are preferred, particularly if the unknown agent is suspected to be a tricyclic antidepressant (TCA) or calcium channel blocker.
- A bedside serum glucose test is mandatory for any patient with altered mental status, as profound neuroglycopenia can perfectly mimic toxic encephalopathy and requires immediate intervention with intravenous dextrose.
- In cases featuring unexplained coma or significant respiratory depression, a diagnostic and therapeutic trial of naloxone is indicated to reverse potential opioid intoxication.

## Clinical Evaluation and History Taking

### Epidemiological and Historical Clues

- The child's age provides critical epidemiological context; exposures in children under $5$ years are predominantly accidental, often involving single agents like common household products, cosmetics, or unsecured pharmaceuticals.
- Conversely, ingestions in the adolescent population are largely intentional, driven by suicide attempts, substance abuse, or misuse, and frequently involve severe polypharmacy.
- A meticulous history should reconstruct the scene: establishing exactly where the child was found (e.g., garage, bathroom), exploring the accessibility of medications belonging to older relatives or visitors, and screening for underlying psychiatric stress or previous suicide attempts in older youths.

### Physical Examination and Toxidromes

- A targeted physical examination is essential to identify classic toxidromes, which are specific constellations of clinical signs that point toward particular pharmacological classes of occult toxins.
- The clinician should carefully assess for characteristic odors on the child's breath or clothing: bitter almonds suggest cyanide; acetone suggests salicylates or isopropyl alcohol; garlic points to organophosphates or arsenic; and mothballs indicate camphor or naphthalene exposure.
- Inspection of the oral cavity can reveal excessive salivation (suggestive of organophosphates or ketamine), oral mucosal burns (indicating caustic or corrosive ingestion), and pigmented gum lines (a hallmark of heavy metal poisoning like lead, mercury, or bismuth).

## Diagnostic and Laboratory Evaluation

- While the diagnosis of poisoning remains primarily clinical, comprehensive laboratory testing is necessary to assess the degree of multi-organ dysfunction and to screen for specific highly toxic occult substances.
- Routine baseline investigations must encompass a complete blood count, serum electrolytes, blood urea nitrogen, serum creatinine, liver transaminases, and blood gas analysis.

### Acid-Base and Osmolar Gap Analysis

- Calculating the anion gap ($Na^+ - [Cl^- + HCO_3^-]$) is a critical step; a high anion gap metabolic acidosis reveals the presence of unmeasured toxic anions.
- Toxins classically responsible for a high anion gap metabolic acidosis include methanol, uremia, diabetic ketoacidosis, paraldehyde, iron, isoniazid, lactic acidosis, ethylene glycol, and salicylates.
- The osmolar gap (measured serum osmolality minus calculated osmolarity) should be determined; an elevated gap ($>10$ mOsm) strongly indicates the presence of unmeasured osmotically active substances, most notably toxic alcohols such as methanol, ethanol, and ethylene glycol.

### Electrocardiography (ECG)

- A $12$-lead ECG is an indispensable bedside tool to rapidly assess for life-threatening cardiotoxicity and specific conduction delays associated with unknown ingestions.
- A widened QRS interval suggests the dangerous blockade of fast sodium channels, a classic finding in severe tricyclic antidepressant toxicity that requires immediate intervention.
- A prolonged QTc interval demonstrates interference with potassium rectifier channels, predisposing the child to torsades de pointes (frequently observed with methadone, phenothiazines, and antipsychotics).

### Specific Toxicology Screening and Radiography

- Quantitative serum screening for acetaminophen and salicylates is strongly recommended in all cases of intentional or unknown poisoning, as early, potentially lethal toxicity may be entirely asymptomatic but is highly amenable to time-sensitive antidotal therapy.
- Urine drugs-of-abuse screens offer limited utility for acute management due to inherent delays and a high rate of false positives and negatives, though they may assist in confirming psychiatric presentations, substance abuse, or child neglect.
- Plain abdominal radiography (KUB) is highly valuable for visualizing specific radiopaque toxins, easily remembered by the mnemonic CHIPPED: Chloral hydrate/Calcium carbonate, Heavy metals (lead, zinc, arsenic), Iron, Phenothiazines, Play-Doh (potassium chloride), Enteric-coated tablets, and Dental amalgam or illicit drug packets.

## Gastrointestinal, Dermal, and Ocular Decontamination

- Decontamination procedures are not universally indicated; their use must be strictly individualized based on the specific properties of the suspected toxin, the route of exposure, the patient's clinical stability, and the time elapsed since the poisoning.
- Dermal and ocular decontamination mandate immediate, extensive flushing of the affected area with tepid water or normal saline for a minimum of $10-20$ minutes.
- Water irrigation must be strictly avoided for exposures to highly reactive compounds, such as elemental sodium, phosphorus, calcium oxide, and titanium tetrachloride.

### Gastric Lavage and Emesis

- The administration of syrup of ipecac is obsolete and contraindicated, as it produces highly variable toxin removal, delays the administration of definitive treatments, and is associated with significant adverse outcomes.
- Gastric lavage is rarely utilized today; it may only be considered within $1$ hour of a massive, potentially lethal ingestion and is strictly contraindicated in patients with an unprotected airway, or those who have ingested corrosive substances or volatile hydrocarbons due to the extreme risk of aspiration and perforation.

### Activated Charcoal (AC)

- Activated charcoal is the single most effective method of gastrointestinal decontamination, achieving maximum efficacy when administered within $1$ hour of toxin ingestion.
- The standard pediatric dose is $1$ g/kg, which demands a completely patent and protected airway prior to administration to prevent catastrophic pulmonary aspiration.
- AC acts through the physical adsorption of toxins onto its highly porous surface; however, it is ineffective at binding heavy metals, iron, lithium, mineral acids, strong alkalis, toxic alcohols, cyanide, and most hydrocarbons.

### Whole-Bowel Irrigation (WBI)

- WBI consists of the enteral instillation of massive volumes of polyethylene glycol electrolyte solution (PEG-ES) via a nasogastric tube at a rate of $35$ mL/kg/hr in children and $1-2$ L/hr in adolescents.
- It is the decontamination method of choice for clearing the gastrointestinal tract of substances poorly adsorbed by charcoal (e.g., iron, heavy metals, lithium), sustained-release pharmaceutical preparations, and illicit drug packets.

## Techniques for Enhanced Elimination

### Urinary Alkalinization

- This pharmacological technique significantly enhances the renal excretion of weak acids by trapping ionized, polar molecules within the renal tubules, thereby preventing their reabsorption into the systemic circulation.
- It is accomplished by administering a continuous intravenous infusion of sodium bicarbonate to maintain a target urine $pH$ of $7.5-8.0$, and serves as the primary elimination strategy for severe salicylate, phenobarbital, and methotrexate toxicity.

### Hemodialysis and Hemofiltration

- Hemodialysis is highly effective for rapidly clearing toxins that possess a low volume of distribution ($<1$ L/kg), low molecular weight, low affinity for plasma proteins, and a high degree of water solubility.
- It is specifically indicated for severe, life-threatening poisonings involving toxic alcohols (methanol, ethylene glycol), lithium, salicylates, theophylline, and barbiturates.

### Multiple-Dose Activated Charcoal (MDAC)

- MDAC accelerates systemic toxin clearance by interrupting the enterohepatic circulation of drugs and utilizing the intestinal mucosa as a membrane for "gastrointestinal dialysis," effectively pulling toxins from the blood back into the gut lumen.
- Administered at a dose of $0.5$ g/kg every $4-6$ hours, MDAC is recommended for life-threatening ingestions of carbamazepine, dapsone, phenobarbital, quinine, and theophylline.

### Intravenous Lipid Emulsion (ILE)

- ILE therapy functions by creating an intravascular "lipid sink" that sequesters highly fat-soluble toxins, thereby reducing their active concentration and mitigating their impact on target organs such as the myocardium.
- It is utilized as a profound, potentially lifesaving intervention in the setting of severe cardiotoxicity secondary to bupivacaine, massive calcium channel blocker overdoses, and tricyclic antidepressant poisonings.

## Diagnostic Trials and Antidotal Therapy

- In the undifferentiated comatose child, the clinical response to specific, carefully titrated antidotes can serve as both a lifesaving therapeutic intervention and a definitive diagnostic tool.
- Naloxone (starting at an intravenous dose of $0.1$ mg/kg) should be administered for presumed opioid toxicity characterized by profound respiratory depression and pinpoint pupils; however, significantly lower doses are mandated if chronic opioid dependence is suspected to prevent the precipitation of severe acute withdrawal.
- Flumazenil ($0.01$ mg/kg) can effectively reverse severe benzodiazepine-induced CNS depression, but it must be used with extreme caution as it carries a substantial risk of unmasking intractable seizures, particularly in patients with suspected co-ingestion of pro-convulsant drugs like TCAs or an underlying seizure disorder.
- For a child exhibiting profound cholinergic signs (DUMBBELS) highly suspicious for organophosphate pesticide poisoning, a diagnostic trial of intravenous atropine ($0.05$ to $0.1$ mg/kg) should be rapidly administered to reverse life-threatening muscarinic symptoms such as severe bradycardia and bronchorrhea.
