## Epidemiology and Classification of Hydrocarbon Poisoning

- Hydrocarbon ingestions are a significant cause of pediatric morbidity, accounting for $\sim 5\%$ of accidental poisonings and $\sim 25\%$ of ingestion-related deaths globally in children $<5$ years of age.
- Young children are frequent accidental victims because these products are often stored inappropriately in unlabeled containers or drinking glasses and possess attractive colors or pleasant smells (e.g., furniture polishes).
- The toxicity and primary organ system involvement depend heavily on the specific chemical classification of the hydrocarbon involved.

|Hydrocarbon Category|Common Examples|Predominant Toxicological Effect|
|:--|:--|:--|
|**Aliphatic**|Kerosene, gasoline, lamp oil, naphtha, mineral spirits|High risk of aspiration pneumonitis and severe lung injury.|
|**Aromatic**|Benzene, toluene|Predominant central nervous system (CNS) toxicity; long-term exposure linked to acute myelogenous leukemia.|
|**Halogenated**|Carbon tetrachloride, methylene chloride, Freon|CNS toxicity, hepatic toxicity, and myocardial sensitization to catecholamines.|

## Pathophysiology of Toxicity

### Pulmonary Injury (Aspiration Pneumonitis)

- The chief and most severe manifestation of aliphatic hydrocarbon toxicity is aspiration pneumonitis.
- Aspiration typically occurs during coughing, gagging, or vomiting at the exact time of ingestion.
- The physical properties of the hydrocarbon dictate its aspiration potential: the risk is inversely proportional to its viscosity and directly proportional to its volatility.
- Compounds with high volatility, low viscosity, and low surface tension (such as kerosene, gasoline, and naphtha) spread rapidly across mucosal surfaces, increasing the likelihood of aspiration into the deep bronchial tree.
- Aspiration of very small quantities ($<1$ mL) can produce widespread and severe lung injury.
- Upon entering the lungs, hydrocarbons inactivate type II pneumocytes, resulting in acute surfactant deficiency, alveolar instability, and early atelectasis.
- Ingestion of hydrocarbons without subsequent aspiration, or isolated dermal absorption, does not directly cause pneumonitis, though it may cause local mucosal irritation.

### Central Nervous System and Cardiovascular Toxicity

- Transient, mild CNS depression is commonly observed following hydrocarbon ingestion or inhalation.
- Aromatic and halogenated hydrocarbons easily cross the blood-brain barrier, leading to direct CNS depression, coma, and seizures.
- Secondary CNS effects (e.g., restlessness, drowsiness, or seizures) may also result from profound hypoxia and metabolic acidosis following severe pulmonary aspiration.
- Halogenated hydrocarbons (which contain chlorine, bromine, or fluorine) can heavily sensitize the myocardium to endogenous catecholamines.
- This myocardial sensitization can lead to refractory ventricular dysrhythmias and sudden cardiovascular collapse, commonly referred to as "sudden sniffing death".

## Clinical Manifestations

- Clinical features vary based on the route of exposure, specific agent, and presence of aspiration.

|System|Clinical Findings and Progression|
|:--|:--|
|**Respiratory**|Symptoms develop early, typically within $6$ hours, secondary to aspiration during ingestion or vomiting. Presentations range from mild cough, tachypnea, and localized wheezing to severe acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) and complete respiratory failure.|
|**Neurological**|Hypoxia and acidosis manifest as restlessness, drowsiness, seizures, and coma. Direct CNS toxicity may cause ataxia, altered mental status, and choreoathetosis.|
|**Cardiovascular**|Tachycardia is common; severe ventricular dysrhythmias are specifically associated with aromatic and halogenated hydrocarbon abuse or exposure.|
|**Systemic / Gastrointestinal**|Poorly absorbed agents like gasoline and kerosene cause considerable direct irritation to the gastrointestinal mucosa, leading to nausea and vomiting. Fever and leukocytosis are very common systemic responses to chemical pneumonitis and do not necessarily imply a bacterial superinfection.|

## Diagnostic and Laboratory Evaluation

### Radiographic Imaging

- A chest radiograph should be performed in all cases of suspected hydrocarbon inhalation or ingestion.
- Initial chest radiographs may appear normal but often demonstrate abnormalities within $6$ hours in patients who have aspirated.
- Classic radiographic findings include bilateral infiltrates, pleural effusions, and signs of ARDS.
- Pneumatoceles (air-filled cystic spaces) are a unique complication that typically appear on chest radiographs $2-3$ weeks after the initial exposure.

### Ancillary Testing

- Arterial Blood Gas (ABG): Crucial for evaluating the degree of hypoxia, hypercapnia, and metabolic acidosis in patients with significant respiratory distress or altered sensorium.
- Electrocardiogram (ECG): Mandatory for identifying cardiac conduction disturbances and ventricular dysrhythmias, particularly if halogenated or aromatic hydrocarbon exposure is suspected.

## Emergency Management and Supportive Care

### Decontamination Protocols

- General surface decontamination must begin immediately by removing all contaminated clothing and washing the skin with soap and water.
- Gastric lavage and the induction of emesis are strictly contraindicated because they dramatically increase the risk of aspiration.
- Cautious evacuation of gastric contents via nasogastric tube is only considered if the hydrocarbon is a vehicle for highly toxic additives, such as pesticides or heavy metals.
- Activated charcoal is not recommended as it does not bind to water-insoluble compounds (like most hydrocarbons) and its administration may induce vomiting, precipitating lethal aspiration.

### Respiratory and Cardiovascular Support

- Management is primarily supportive and focuses heavily on aggressive respiratory care.
- Supplemental oxygen should be administered to correct hypoxia, and beta-agonists may be utilized to manage bronchospasm and wheezing.
- Patients developing ARDS or severe respiratory failure may require intubation and mechanical ventilation. In refractory cases, high-frequency ventilation or extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) may be indicated.
- Routine administration of corticosteroids or prophylactic antibiotics is not recommended, as they have shown no clinical benefit in improving outcomes from chemical pneumonitis.
- If ventricular dysrhythmias occur secondary to halogenated hydrocarbon inhalation, they should be managed with beta-blockers (e.g., esmolol) to specifically counteract the sensitization of the myocardium to catecholamines.

### Clinical Approach and Disposition Algorithm

- All patients with a reliable history of hydrocarbon ingestion must be observed in a medical facility for a minimum of $6$ hours, regardless of their initial clinical status.

|Patient Status at Presentation|Intervention and Disposition Pathway|
|:--|:--|
|**Asymptomatic**|Remove clothing and wash skin. Perform a baseline chest radiograph. Observe strictly for $6$ hours. If the patient remains completely asymptomatic and the chest radiograph remains normal, discharge with assured follow-up.|
|**Symptomatic (Wheezing, Tachypnea, Altered Sensorium)**|Admit the patient. Administer oxygen, intravenous fluids, and beta-agonists. Continuously monitor respiratory and neurologic status. Do not administer steroids or prophylactic antibiotics. Obtain serial chest radiographs to monitor the progression of pneumonitis or ARDS.|
