## Introduction and Epidemiology

Williams syndrome is a rare, multisystem neurodevelopmental microdeletion disorder with an estimated incidence of 1 in 7,500 to 10,000 live births. It occurs equally in both sexes and across all ethnic groups.

## Molecular Genetics and Pathogenesis

The condition is caused by a heterozygous contiguous gene deletion (1.5 to 1.8 megabases) on the long arm of chromosome 7 at locus 7q11.23. The vast majority of cases (>99%) occur sporadically due to a de novo meiotic recombination error during gametogenesis.

|Gene Involved|Pathophysiological Consequence|
|:--|:--|
|**ELN (Elastin)**|Haploinsufficiency causes connective tissue abnormalities, including supravalvular aortic stenosis (SVAS), peripheral pulmonary stenosis, joint laxity, premature skin wrinkling, and hoarse voice.|
|**GTF2I and GTF2IRD1**|Implicated in the distinctive neurocognitive profile, visuospatial deficits, and craniofacial dysmorphism.|
|**LIMK1**|Associated with impaired visual-motor integration and visuospatial constructive cognition.|
|**NCF1**|Paradoxically decreases the risk of developing hypertension when included in atypical larger deletions.|

## Clinical Manifestations

|System|Clinical Features|
|:--|:--|
|**Craniofacial**|"Elfin facies": broad forehead, bitemporal narrowing, periorbital fullness, epicanthal folds, stellate or lacy iris pattern, short nose with anteverted nares, malar hypoplasia, long smooth philtrum, wide mouth with full lips.|
|**Cardiovascular**|SVAS (present in up to 75%), peripheral pulmonary stenosis, hypertension (affecting ~50% of children and majority of adults), and thickened coronary arterial walls increasing ischemia risk.|
|**Neurocognitive**|Mild mental retardation (average IQ ~50), engaging extrovert ("cocktail party") personality, good verbal and language skills contrasting with poor mathematical and visuospatial abilities, generalized anxiety, and hyperacusis.|
|**Endocrine**|Neonatal hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and hypothyroidism.|
|**Gastrointestinal**|Severe colic and feeding difficulties in infancy, gastroesophageal reflux disease, chronic constipation, diverticulitis, and rectal prolapse.|
|**Renal**|Structural abnormalities, renal artery stenosis, and nephrocalcinosis.|
|**Musculoskeletal**|Generalized joint laxity in early childhood progressing to contractures, kyphoscoliosis, radioulnar synostosis, and small widely spaced peg-shaped teeth.|

## Diagnostic Evaluation

- **Chromosomal Microarray (CMA):** Primary diagnostic test confirming the 7q11.23 microdeletion and defining the exact size and boundary.
- **Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH):** Standard confirmatory test using an elastin (ELN) specific probe.
- **Baseline Clinical Investigations:** Urgent 4-limb blood pressure measurement, comprehensive echocardiography with Doppler flow studies, serum calcium (ionized and total), urine calcium-to-creatinine ratio, thyroid function tests, and renal ultrasonography with Doppler.

## Management Principles

- **Cardiovascular Care:** Serial clinical exams and echocardiograms are mandatory. Surgical correction (e.g., patch aortoplasty) is indicated for hemodynamically significant SVAS.
- **Hypertension Management:** Aggressive medical management using calcium channel blockers or angiotensin receptor blockers.
- **Anesthesia Warning:** Extreme caution is required during general anesthesia due to a significantly increased risk of sudden cardiac death from decreased coronary perfusion in the setting of SVAS and biventricular hypertrophy.
- **Endocrine and GI Care:** Management of hypercalcemia often requires dietary restriction of calcium and vitamin D. Osmotic laxatives are used for chronic constipation.
- **Long-Term Prognosis:** Ultimate prognosis depends on the severity of cardiovascular involvement. The majority of adults require lifelong supportive care and supervised living arrangements due to intellectual disability and psychiatric comorbidities.
